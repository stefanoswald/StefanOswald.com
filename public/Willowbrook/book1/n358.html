Night 358

Mama's Letter

Month 12: The Candle's Country

The kitchen's latest hour, the house asleep at its every documented floor, the lamp at its lowest working gold, held at seven nights' term the almanac's own commissioned page: Mama's letter, written at the tradition's founding edition, to be read per its own text at some far volume's term: when you're big enough to ask.

The page enters the letter at the record's discretion, per the household's permission, entire.

"Dear Olivia. Livy, you said this week, at the mirror, and I wrote it down with the date, because I write everything down now; you taught me that; you taught me most of it.

You are almost one. You are asleep upstairs with both arms over your head, the way you've slept since the swaddle came off, the way I suspect you'll sleep forever, all in, holding nothing back, even from sleeping.

I want to tell you what the year was, because you won't remember it, and because the album shows the smiles but not the rest, and the rest is the part I most want you to have. The rest was: I didn't know how. Nobody knows how. You arrived and the whole world tilted and I stood in this kitchen at three in the morning more times than the almanac admits, sure I was doing it wrong. And here is what I learned, and it's the only thing I know for certain now: the doing-it-wrong feeling is the doing-it-right. The ones who worry are the ones who show up. I showed up. Your papa showed up, oh, Livy, how that man shows up; ask him about the seed catalog sometime. And you, you showed up most of all: every morning, delighted, ready, as if we'd never once gotten it wrong. You forgave us the whole year in advance. That's what babies do. It's the most generous thing I've ever been given.

You said duck at the pond and home at the willow and you kissed my finger better and I want you to know: it was better. It's all been better. Every single thing, since June-before-last.

Tomorrow-week you turn one, and the book says every arrival is also a departure, and the book is right. I've been waving at both all year. So here's my wave at this one: goodbye, my baby. Hello, my little girl. I was there for all of it. I'd do every three a.m. again for free.

Life doesn't happen to us, little one. It happens for us. You happened for me.

Mama"

The letter was folded at the lamp's term, envelope-sealed per the curl's own precedent, and berthed at the keepsake harbor's deepest documented shelf: for Livy, when she asks.

Seven nights. The letter: written, sealed, harbored.

The tradition, the page certifies at the whole shelf's forecast, founded: one letter, every candle, at every volume's own latest kitchen hour, for the asking years ahead.

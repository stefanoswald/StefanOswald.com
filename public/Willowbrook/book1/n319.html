Night 319

Bobby Bear's Fourth Letter

Month 11 — Lantern Season

Dear Olivia,

Fourth letter. Last one, probably, from this address — and B. Bear, Dresser Division, has never in his commission had so much to report.

You walk. You've walked for months now, but I want it in the record in my own paw: I watched the first one, Wednesday before lunch, three feet into your mother's catch, from the best seat in the house. The dresser sees everything. That is the whole point of a dresser, and I have kept it faithfully.

You talk. You call me Baba, which I have checked against the older bears' registries and which is, per the archives, a promotion. You say hi to me at dusk and nigh-nigh at the lamp, and last Tuesday you stood at my dresser and delivered what I can only file as a briefing — four minutes, six coins, one full phrase, and a conviction I have not seen since the morning addresses of your crib era. I understood every word. Bears do. It is our second whole point.

You fed me, in October. Nothing was in the cup. I have never been so full in my life.

And now the news from my own desk: the orders came through. You'll be one, in six weeks and a bit — I hear everything from the moon, who cosigns all my paperwork — and at one year old, per the safe-sleep statutes I have honored since your first night home (bare crib, boring and perfect; I guarded it from here and I'd do it again), the embargo lifts. The Dresser Division closes. I move in.

I want you to know I have been training. I am soft, but I have been practicing being softer. I have studied your grip — the certified one, the pincer, the ear-seizing edition — and I am told my left ear is the flopped one for reasons of destiny. I have reviewed the drool archives of your teething seasons and I am prepared, structurally and spiritually. The patchwork heart on my foot has been re-stitched by your mother — she thought nobody saw; the dresser sees everything — so it will hold at the coming decades' documented rates.

Because that is the commission now, little one, and I have read it twice: not the watching. The being-held. The nights of the next books entire — the ones where I ride the fevers with you, and the first days of school, and the fort navies, and the long stories your father does the voices for. There is a rumor in the older registries — the moon hums it some nights — that in the far volumes I get up and walk around in your dreams, and later than that, further down the shelf, that I get handed one day to somebody smaller, some green-starred somebody, when you're done needing me — which is, per the archives, how bears retire: never.

Six weeks, and a little more. I have the watch until then. I have loved having the watch.

Then you'll have me. All of it — the flopped ear, the re-stitched heart, the whole soft commission — yours.

Sleep well, walker. Talk to me at dusk. I hear everything.

— B. Bear, Dresser Division (closing), Crib Division (pending), Yours (permanent)

The moon countersigned, per procedure. The countersignature, the record shows, took a moment longer than the paperwork required.

Forty-six nights. The orders: posted. The heart: re-stitched, and holding.

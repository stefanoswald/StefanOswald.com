Night 15

The Team of Two

Month 1: Arrival

Two shipwrecked sailors cannot both bail the boat every hour of every night. They will only sink slower.

So Mama and Papa drew up the oldest treaty in parenthood: shifts. Mama took the first watch and slept early; Papa owned the ten o'clock feed with a bottle, so one of them always got one long, unbroken stretch. Weekends, they traded mornings, one slept shamelessly late while the other and Olivia held a very quiet party downstairs.

And they made the harder promise too: to say the numbers out loud. "I'm at a two tonight." "I've got her, go to bed." No scorekeeping, no whose-turn arithmetic at three a.m., when all arithmetic turns mean.

A baby does not need two perfect parents. She needs two people who take turns being the rested one.

The handoff in the hallway became its own small ceremony. A pass of the warm bundle, a kiss on a tired cheek, a whispered "we're doing it."

They were, too.

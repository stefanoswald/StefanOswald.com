Night 230

The Spring Fleet Review

Month 8: The Velcro Weeks and the Thaw

The thaw reached the interior on the year's first open-window Saturday, and the household ran the season's traditional exercise: the fleet review, every toy, book, and apparatus mustered on the living room floor for inspection, reassignment, or honorable discharge.

The discharges, processed first, each with its small ceremony of the shelf. The newborn rattles, unshaken since autumn. The play gym, that first arena of the batting era, its felt star swatted into venerable shabbiness, basement-bound with the bassinet and the little tub, the keepsake fleet growing by the season. The wrist-rattles, the crinkle book's gummed first edition, the pacifiers of the early administration (retired at the six-month line per the household's own weaning of them, and kept, the way houses keep such things, in a drawer nobody discusses).

The promotions and transfers, next. The rotation baskets, the three-toys doctrine's infrastructure, holding since the fifth month, restocked for the coming seas: the putting-in industries expanded (cups, tins, the posting canister's second edition with a wider slot and new cargo), the push wagon's freight division chartered, the stacking rings promoted from gnawing stock to actual stacking prospect, the board-book shelf culled of the outgrown and reinforced with the toddler canon's vanguard. The bear, it goes without saying, reviewed nothing and answered to no muster; he held the dresser per his commission, one ear flopped, above all inventories.

And the review's true cargo, the page notes, was never the toys. It was the audit riding along, every discharged object a milestone certificate, every promotion a forecast, and the hour the parents spent on the floor among the fleet, handling the year's material history piece by piece: the rattle that was the whole world in July, the cups that taught the ocean, the felt star with the wear of ten thousand swats. The almanac calls such hours stocktaking and schedules them deliberately at the seasons' turns, and the page co-signs the practice for every household: the growing goes by in the daily blur, per the train-window truth of the first month; the fleet, mustered, makes it visible all at once.

The basement shelf received its spring consignment. The baskets, restocked, resumed their rotation.

And one item crossed the review uncategorizable, flagged by both auditors and returned to service without discussion: the crackle paper's ceremonial remnant, soft as cloth now, six months a fleet member, outlasting its third catalog cohort.

Some commissions, the review concluded, are permanent.

The window stayed open all afternoon. The spring came in over the whole reviewed fleet, and the fleet, trimmed and ready, awaited its sailor's next season.

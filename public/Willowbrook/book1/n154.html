Night 154

Gag Is Not Choke

Month 6 — The Feast Begins

Day four of the feast delivered the moment every feeding parent meets, and this page exists so the meeting goes as it should.

A lump — soft, legal, properly sized — wandered rearward ahead of schedule, and Olivia gagged: the cough-splutter, the flushed face, the tongue thrusting the offender forward, the whole system executing its loud and vigorous defense. Four seconds, start to finish. The lump returned to the front office. The diner grinned, damp, and reapplied for more.

Mama, by force of rehearsed will, had done the hardest thing in feeding: nothing. Stayed level, stayed watchful, let the machinery run.

The distinction, printed here in the book's plainest type: gagging is loud, choking is quiet. The gag — noisy, red-faced, sputtering, recovering — is the guardian working, placed far forward on an infant's tongue precisely for this training season, and triggered often and harmlessly as the mouth maps its new trade. The choke is the opposite country: silence, or a wrong small sound; no cry; the face going wide-eyed or dusky; nothing moving. The gag wants calm witness. The choke wants action, that instant, by hands that learned the infant maneuvers before the feast began — the class taken, this house's counsel, by every adult who will ever hold the spoon; an evening's course, a lifetime's equipment.

The tabletop laws that keep gags mere gags rode in the margin: seated always, upright always, strapped, attended, unhurried; no laughing mid-mouthful, no eating on the move; and the forbidden list posted plain — whole grapes, whole nuts, hard rounds of anything, the classics of the choke chart, exiled until the toddler years cut them lengthwise into legality.

The guardian drilled twice more that week, to decreasing reviews.

The witness stayed level each time, which was the entire assignment, and the hardest one on the syllabus.

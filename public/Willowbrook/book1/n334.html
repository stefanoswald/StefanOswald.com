Night 334

Stella at the Window, Early

Month 11 — Lantern Season

On the month's last night, at the hour the town's lights banked to their embers, Stella broke — for the first documented time in the whole year's watch — her own protocol.

The watch's standing terms, per the registry the sky keeps: the post held at altitude, the reports taken at the willow's branch, the window's file read from the winter station's proper remove — the distance being, per the watch's own founding statutes, the office: stars keep; they do not visit.

But the month's last night ran cold and clear at the frost's own polish, and the file below ran thirty-one nights wide, and the star — the page records it per the town's discretion, without explanation, per Willowbrook — came down.

Low. Lower than the harvest session's amber, lower than the peepers' spring handover, low enough that her gold silvered the nursery sill itself: the small correct darkness at one pane's remove, the whole watched year at arm's term. The crib at its lowest setting, per the audit's compiled clauses. The bear at his closing division's last posted weeks, one ear flopped at the moonlight, the re-stitched heart at attention. The sleeper at her starfish term, one fist at the sleep sack's seam, the twelve-month country folded down around her per the winter's own quilt — the walker, the talker, the gentle-certified, the home-sayer: the whole file, breathing, at four counts a minute's parental arithmetic, entire.

"I watched you arrive," said Stella, at the register stars use through glass, which no instrument has ever caught and every sleeping child has heard. "June-before-last, the low warm three a.m. light — I filed it myself: a new one. And in thirty-one nights they light your candle, and the next book opens, and the watch — this is what I came down to say; stars should say some things at the sill — the watch doesn't end at the candle. It's yours for the whole shelf. Every volume. I've read ahead."

The sleeper held her term, per the file. One fist opened, at some dream's own paperwork, and closed.

"Yes," said Stella, per the translation the whole year had certified. "Exactly. Sleep on, walker."

She rose at the protocol's restored altitude, the sill's silver folding back to frost, the post retaken at the winter station's proper term — and the Sleepy Moon, who had watched the entire visit at the hum's own discretion, said nothing at all, which is Moon for some breaks in protocol are the protocol, oldest one on the books; well kept, small colleague; well kept all year.

Thirty-one nights down to the candle in the town of Willowbrook.

The eleventh month: closed. The last one: at the sill.

Goodnight, walker. The watch — per the visit's own filed word — whole-shelf, permanent, yours.

Goodnight, Willowbrook.

Night 175

The Lap Spring

Month 6 — The Feast Begins

The lap developed a spring in it this month, and the spring had plans.

Held standing on Papa's knees — her grip on his thumbs like flight controls — Olivia no longer stood. She bounced: knees dipping, legs firing, the whole undercarriage pistoning in a rhythm nobody taught, grinning at the ceiling on every launch. Minutes of it. Sets and reps. A small stevedore working the evening shift, delighted by her own machinery.

The old wives' caution — that early standing bows the legs — had reached the house by aunt, as it reaches every house, and the page retires it here with the pediatric establishment's full blessing: a baby bouncing on a lap she chose, bearing what her own frame invites, is doing the strength curriculum, not violating it. The bowing rumor confuses this with the propping of babies who haven't asked; the ask is the whole distinction. Legs that fire want firing. The spring is the syllabus.

What the spring was building had a schedule attached, and the almanac took the forecast soberly: pulling to stand, the coming quarter's headline, the crib rail's destiny visible from here (the mattress was lowered that weekend, pre-emptively, the page's standing advice — lower it at the first lap-spring, not the first pull, cribs being scaled by climbers without notice). Then cruising, the furniture coastline. Then the unthinkable, thinkable now, somewhere past the year line or before it.

None of which concerned the stevedore, who worked her shift for the shift's own wage: up, down, the thumbs steady, the ceiling grinned at, the biggest safest laugh in the house rumbling underneath her through his knees.

"She's going to be trouble," said Papa, meaning the opposite, meaning the future, meaning yes.

Bounce, agreed the spring. Bounce, bounce, bounce.

The floor could wait. The floor, the record notes, would not wait long.

Night 131

The Guilt Hour

Month 5: Nearly Sitting, Nearly Everything

The guilt kept office hours, and its office was the hallway at ten p.m.

That was where it found Mama most nights now, hand on the doorframe after the last look-in, tallying. The morning she had wished, for one hot second, to be anywhere else. The work email answered during floor time. The Tuesday freedom, enjoyed, enjoyed! while her own child laughed in another house. The bedtime song rushed on Thursday because she was simply, humanly, done.

This page keeps the hallway's counsel, and it is brief.

The guilt is not evidence. It is love, misfiled, the same attention that reads the sleepy cues and packs the diaper bag, turned inward and set to grade. It arrives heaviest not in the failing parents but in the trying ones; the ones who should worry never do. Its claims dissolve under one honest audit: fed, held, answered, delighted in, the real checklist, ticked daily, in this house since the first night home.

And the wished-for elsewhere, the savored Tuesday, the finished coffee? Those are not leaks in the love. They are its maintenance schedule. A mother is a person keeping a person; both persons require keeping.

So Mama ran the audit, there in her office hours. Checklist: ticked. Case: dismissed. Again tomorrow, likely; the office reopens nightly for a while, for everyone.

She closed the nursery door to its practiced inch.

Down the hall, the moon had laid its path on the floorboards, silver and unbothered, the way it lights all the hallways of all the trying parents, every night, on its rounds.

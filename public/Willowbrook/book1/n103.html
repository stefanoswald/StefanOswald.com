Night 103

Hats and the Summer Sun

Month 4 — Rolling and Laughing

Summer arrived in Willowbrook wearing its whole outfit, and Olivia's first hot season came with rules of engagement.

Rule one, the iron rule of the first six months: her skin and the direct sun were not to meet. Not via lotion — sunscreen waits, mostly, for the six-month line, save a dab in emergencies — but via the older technologies: shade, timing, and cloth. The stroller canopy at full sail. The picnic blanket pitched in tree shadow, moved as the shadow moved, like slow chess with the oak. Outings steered around the fierce middle hours, morning and late afternoon owning the calendar now.

Rule two: the hat. Wide-brimmed, chin-strapped, purchased in optimism.

The hat war began at once, as hat wars do. Hat on; hat seized and gummed; hat on; hat gone overboard. The counterinsurgency ran on the standard doctrine — calm re-application without fanfare, distraction deployed at the moment of donning, parents visibly wearing their own hats, solidarity being the only argument babies respect.

By July's end, détente: the hat stayed on if the duck came along. All wars end in trade agreements.

Light cotton layers, a watch on the cheeks for pink, extra feeds in the heat — milk being drink and food and air conditioning all at once — and the summer was not an enemy at all, just a bright country with border formalities.

Under the oak, in the moving shade, the hatted ambassador slept through her first heat wave entirely.

The sun, respectfully, kept its distance. The paperwork was in order.

Night 146

Duchess and the Deputy

Month 5 — Nearly Sitting, Nearly Everything

Sunday's fence-line constitutional produced, this week, a diplomatic incident, and the almanac would want it recorded.

The Duchess — the speckled hen of complicated situation — had never before acknowledged the deputy. Hens acknowledge little; it is their policy. But this Sunday, as Grandpa Joe held Olivia at the wire and ran the introductions, the Duchess broke protocol: approached, halted one regal pace off, and inspected the baby with a full slow turn of one amber eye.

Olivia, five months schooled in the reaching arts, extended her hand — and here the training told. Not the grab; the fence and the weeks of watching had taught the terms. The hand went out flat, open, still: the fifth-month version of a formal address.

Hen and deputy regarded each other across the wire for a long moment of interspecies procedure.

"Well now," said Grandpa Joe, which from him was a state dinner.

The page beneath the comedy: animals are the year's best teachers of a lesson nothing else teaches as well — that other creatures are selves, with terms, approached on the terms or not at all. The baby who learns waiting from a hen, calm from an old dog, distance from a cat's raised tail, is banking the original diplomacy, the one all later kindness is built on. Farms and fence lines teach it wholesale; a town sidewalk teaches it retail; it is never not on the syllabus.

The Duchess concluded the audience with one dismissive shake and returned to her constituency.

The deputy pumped both arms — received at court! — and the fence line resumed its slow parade home.

"She'll remember you now," Grandpa Joe advised. Whether he meant the hen or the baby, the record does not show. The record suspects both.

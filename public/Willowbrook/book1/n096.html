Night 96

Everything Is a Teether

Month 4 — Rolling and Laughing

The gums declared a state of emergency in the fourth month, and everything within reach was conscripted.

The wooden ring: gummed. The cloth block: gummed. Papa's knuckle, Mama's chin, the bear's ear during a security lapse, the edge of the good blanket, and once, ambitiously, the entire mirror: gummed, gummed, gummed.

Was it teething? The eternal question, and the honest answer of the fourth month: possibly, eventually, who can say. Real teeth might be weeks out or months; first teeth commonly surface around six or seven months, and the fuss-to-tooth pipeline is famously long and famously foggy. What was certain was the itch — gums doing groundwork, pressure that wanted counter-pressure — and the relief protocol, which works whatever the timeline.

Cold, but never frozen: the silicone ring from the refrigerator door, a washcloth corner chilled and offered. Texture: bumps and ridges outperform smooth. Pressure: a clean parental finger, firmly rubbed along the ridge, received like a professional service. And the drool infrastructure scaled up again — bibs in rotation, chin patted dry, balm at the first pink.

Everything else stayed the toilet-tube law and common sense: nothing small, nothing brittle, nothing on strings.

Olivia worked the chilled ring like a craftsman, sighing through her nose.

Somewhere below the pink horizon, the teeth took their time, as teeth do.

The emergency was managed. The blanket, however, would never fully recover.

Night 145

The Almanac of Normal

Month 5 — Nearly Sitting, Nearly Everything

The notebook had started as a feeding log in the blurred first weeks — times, ounces, sides, survival arithmetic. Somewhere along the months it had become something else, and tonight, with Olivia asleep across his knees like a warm paperweight, Papa read the almanac back.

Week 2: cries decoded, badly. Week 3: the growth spurt; nobody warned us; now we warn everyone. Week 6: peak crying, right on the books' schedule — the books were right, tell the books. Week 9, in Mama's hand: SHE SMILED. Underlined past the paper's tolerance. Week 14: the renovation; three weeks; we lived. Week 17: BA. Week 20, this week, his own entry pending: backward under the coffee table, indignant. Ask the almanac: do all ships launch sternward?

The page makes its case for the habit itself. Not the curated baby book with the doves on it — the working almanac, kept in the kitchen, entries at found moments in whichever hand was free. Its uses compound: the fog-cutter (was the last regression really three weeks? — the almanac knows, when memory swears it was ten), the pattern-catcher (fussy Thursdays traced, once, to Wednesday's late nap), the question-keeper for doctor visits, and the deep archive — because the first year erases itself as it runs, the days blurring like the train-window landscape, and the almanac is the only camera that catches the ordinary.

And in some far year, this page predicts, the notebook becomes the family's dearest book: the era readable again, in two tired handwritings, week by astonished week.

He added the entry. Sternward, indignant, magnificent.

The paperweight sighed, holding all the pages down at once.

Night 225

The Brush, the Phone, the Mirror

Month 8 — The Velcro Weeks and the Thaw

The imitation department, double-shifted since the cousin's visit, opened three new bureaus this month, and the household surveilled them with the delight the era's comedy is owed.

The brush bureau, first: the hairbrush, liberated from the dresser during a routine raid, was carried not to the mouth — the customs office, for once, waived inspection — but to the head. Her own head. Brushed, after a fashion: the bristles backward half the time, the stroke more salute than groom, the intent unmistakable. This is what the tall ones do with this object, and I am of the tall ones' tribe. The bureau expanded within the week: the brush applied to Mama's head (gently), to the bear's head (thoroughly, the patchwork heart receiving a full salon service), to Papa's beard (a professional challenge, met).

The phone bureau, second, and the month's finest theater: any object of roughly the sacred proportions — the toy phone, the remote, the banana at breakfast, one wooden block — pressed to the ear, and the conversation commenced: the babble chains delivered in the unmistakable cadence of telephone talk, pauses held for the invisible interlocutor, the head nodding per the observed custom, and the sign-off — the object regarded, then dropped, the call's end as observed from the receiving end her whole life.

And the mirror bureau, quietly the deepest: the mirror baby, that faithful colleague since month three, now received performances — the wave delivered to her, the clap, the pat-a-cake attempted against the glass — the friend still unrecognized as self (that sky-opening waits past the first birthday, per the old page's promise) but engaged now as audience, the social repertoire rehearsed on the world's most agreeable colleague.

The page files the bureaus under their true heading: imitation is the tribe's tuition-free university, and its enrollment is total now. Everything performed before her is curriculum — the brushing, the calling, the stirring, the sweeping, the kiss on the cheek at the door (attempted this month on the bear: the almanac's tenderest entry, unimproveable) — and the parental art is merely to perform the world worth copying, slowly, at watchable range, per the custom the book has kept since the narrated documentaries of month two.

The evening call, taken on the banana at dinner, ran two full minutes of cadenced babble and concluded with the observed sign-off.

Whoever was on the line, the family agreed, got the full report.

They usually do, at that bureau. The world, watched, was being learned at capacity. The tribe's newest member was studying, at every window, for all of it.

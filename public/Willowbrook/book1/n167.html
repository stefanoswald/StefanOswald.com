Night 167

Babble Goes to Church

Month 6 — The Feast Begins

The Thursday library run had become liturgy — three board books out, three returned, the lantern lit above as the season darkened — and this Thursday, in the vaulted hush of the children's corner, Olivia discovered acoustics.

"BA." Pause. The room gave it back, rounder, from the high wood ceiling. Her face went through discovery's whole parade. "BA-BA-DA." The vault returned the chain with cathedral manners. "MA-MA-BA-GRRR—" and she was off, delivering to the reading dome the most resonant address of her career, volume climbing with revelation, while Mama performed the parent's oldest liturgical dance: pride and shush in one gesture.

The librarian, consulted by apologetic eyebrow, waved it off with the serenity of her order. "That's language happening," she said. "We're pro-language here."

The page seconds the librarian, and files the science beneath the comedy. Babble in the sixth and seventh months is entering its canonical phase — reduplicated chains, the ba-ba-ba and ma-ma-ma of the textbooks — and its volume experiments are the point: loud, soft, squealed, whispered, the young voice mapping its own instrument's range against every room it meets. New acoustics are new data. The bathroom concert, the stairwell address, the library vault — each a different answer to the standing question what does my voice do here, and the baby who asks it everywhere is running the vocal syllabus at full enrollment.

(The whisper, the page notes, lands months later than the shout, self-regulation trailing discovery as ever. Until then, the parental shush is choreography, not enforcement, and libraries staffed by the wise know it.)

From the wainscoting gap by the low shelf, unobserved as always, Molly Mouse checked the young patron's progress against her private records and found it excellent: loud, curious, book-adjacent. The library's future, in her professional view, was secure.

Three new books were stamped. The address concluded on the steps, open-air encore, to scattered applause from two passing porches.

The lantern glowed them home through the first early dusk of autumn. Some sermons are one syllable long, repeated with conviction. The town heard this one entire.

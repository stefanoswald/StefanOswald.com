Night 120

The Fireflies Report to the Moon

Month 4: Rolling and Laughing

On summer nights, the moon takes its reports from the fireflies, who see everything low down and miss nothing that glows.

They rose from the lawns of Willowbrook in their slow unhurried sparks and told the Sleepy Moon what the season knew. That the baby on the porch swing had reached for eleven of them tonight and caught none and laughed anyway, the correct response to fireflies, they noted, and rarer in grown people than it should be. That her laugh had a new gear in it. That she had said ba to the night in general, and the night had taken it kindly, as the night takes all first words.

Stella, arriving for her shift, confirmed the low-altitude findings and added the household file: renovation complete, architecture holding; three naps, more or less; one father discovered to be a cello; one hundred nights of stories passed and marked with a stone.

"The fourth month," Stella summarized, "is when they start reaching for lights."

"Mmmmm," hummed the Sleepy Moon, which is Moon for and they never really stop, if it goes well. Look at you. Look at the fireflies. Everyone up here was once reached for.

The fireflies blinked their agreement in the old code and sank back toward the lawns.

Below, the porch light clicked off. The nursery lamp glowed, dimmed, died. The town tucked itself in, street by street.

Stella twinkled once, for yes.

Four months down in the town of Willowbrook. All lights accounted for.

Goodnight, Willowbrook.

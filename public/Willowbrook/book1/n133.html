Night 133

The Bib Drawer

Month 5 — Nearly Sitting, Nearly Everything

The bib drawer had reached institutional size, and this page pays it brief, damp tribute.

It had begun modestly — three bibs, gifts, decorative. The drool era's economics had done the rest: the terry workhorses bought in eight-packs, the bandana style that made her look like a very small train robber, the plastic-backed heavy weather gear, the two fancy ones reserved for photographs and ruined at the first photograph. A taxonomy, assembled by necessity, rotated like crops.

The tribute is practical, as tributes in this book tend to be. The bib is the fifth month's least glamorous and highest-yield technology: one snap of cloth converting the day from four soaked outfits to one dry one, from chest rash to clear skin, from laundry mountain to laundry hill. Cotton against the skin, the waterproof layer for mealtime's coming era, the snap or velcro that survives a one-handed change — small engineering, honed by generations of wet chins.

And the drawer taught the drawer's lesson, the one that scales: parenting's real infrastructure is mostly this humble. Not the heirloom crib but the burp cloths; not the beautiful toys but the rotation basket; not the parenting library but the washcloth in the icebox. The unphotographed fleet, doing the actual carrying.

Olivia wore today's selection — the train robber model, red — with her customary indifference to fashion and total commitment to soaking it by noon.

The drawer stood ready with reinforcements, eleven deep.

Institutional. Damp. Essential. The tribute stands.

Night 112

The Pillow Court

Month 4 — Rolling and Laughing

A throne was constructed on the nursery rug: pillows in a horseshoe, the firm one at the back, the realm carpeted in blanket.

Olivia was installed upon it, and the pillow court convened.

She sat — after a fashion. The fourth-month version of sitting is a negotiation with gravity conducted in real time: the torso tall for three proud seconds, then the slow lean, the correction, the over-correction, the sideways settle into cushions engineered for exactly this diplomacy. And lately, the breakthrough the court gathered to witness: the hands, planted forward on the rug like two small pillars, holding the whole parliament upright. The tripod sit, the books call it — arriving for most babies in the fifth and sixth months, rehearsing now in pillowed sessions like this.

The rules of court were three. Sessions short, ended before the wobble turned to woe. The realm padded but the floor honest — cushions for the fall, not instead of practice, since the falls are the tuition and the catching is the course. And no propping to perfection: a baby wedged bolt upright learns the throne; a baby allowed to wobble learns the balance, which travels.

Session today: four minutes, two topples, one historic stretch of nine unsupported seconds during which the entire court held its breath.

Adjourned by yawn, carried to the crib still faintly regal.

The pillows kept the throne warm. The balance, meanwhile, kept the appointment — it always keeps the appointment.

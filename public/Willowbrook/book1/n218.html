Night 218

The Cheerio Standard

Month 8 — The Velcro Weeks and the Thaw

The crane, previewed in the sixth month and commissioned through the seventh, reached in the eighth its full operating standard — and the industry's benchmark unit, here as in every high chair in the hemisphere, was the round oat cereal.

The Cheerio (the page uses the vernacular; the almanac logs the generic) is the pincer's perfect examination: small enough to demand the true thumb-and-forefinger grip, the rake disqualified by physics; forgiving enough to survive imperfect capture; dissolving enough to retire the choke committee's concerns; and — the engineering detail no toy achieves — holed, the aperture adding a targeting challenge the eight-month mind found frankly delicious.

The breakfast trials ran daily and the data sang. Capture rate, month's start: perhaps one in three, the misses skidding the disc across the tray to the floor's waiting audit. Month's end: near-total, the grip descending with a jeweler's economy, thumb and forefinger closing on the rim at first pass. Transit: mouthward, direct, the old fist-detours retired. And the advanced curriculum, self-assigned per the age's custom: the transfer of a captured disc to the other hand's custody while the pincer re-deployed; the deposit of one disc into the water cup (research, presumably, into cereal navigation); and the month's crowning integration — one disc, pinched, extended, and delivered into Papa's opened mouth, the gift economy and the fine-motor curriculum merging in a single soggy-free transaction, received with ceremony and requested again by opened mouth, a game instantly canonized as breakfast's finest.

The page's counsel holds from the puff era, upgraded: the tray as training bench, targets in constellations of four or five, misses unassisted, the floor's losses budgeted as tuition. And a note for the watching pride: the pincer now standard on cereal would generalize within weeks to everything its owner encountered — the lint on the carpet (customs office, alerted), the crumb under the couch (audit, tightened), the one pebble every spring sidewalk offers (the walk's new pace: archaeological).

The world, it turned out, was made almost entirely of very small things, and she could pick up all of them now.

She did. Every one. For months.

The vacuum's job security, the almanac noted, had never looked better. Neither, it added, had hers.

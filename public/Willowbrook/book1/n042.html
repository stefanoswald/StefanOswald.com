Night 42

The Black and White Gallery

Month 2 — Smiles and Sleep

The art gallery opened on the living room rug, and every piece in the collection was black and white.

Stripes. Checkerboards. A spiral that meant business. Mama propped the cards eight, ten inches from Olivia's face, and the connoisseur went silent, staring with her whole body, arms paused mid-wave — the deep drink of a brain feeding.

New eyes are hungry for edges. Color vision is still warming up in the second month, so the loudest thing you can show a young baby is contrast: black on white, bold and simple. It is not decoration; it is exercise. Every hard edge she studies is a bench press for the seeing parts of her brain.

The gallery had a second wing, better than any card: faces. Faces are a baby's favorite pattern in the universe — the dark of eyes, the line of a hairline, the moving mouth that makes the sounds.

So the show ended the way all shows should. Cards down, Mama's face in close, and the critic gave her review: the brand-new smile, five stars, would look again.

Gallery hours: whenever awake. Admission: free forever.

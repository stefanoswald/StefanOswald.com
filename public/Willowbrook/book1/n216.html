Night 216

The Restaurant, Unchaperoned

Month 8: The Velcro Weeks and the Thaw

The overnight bag question had been answered in the fall by an evening at the farm. The eighth month raised the harder, nearer variant: the babysitter, the actual restaurant, the town's own Friday, the first true night out since the door on Maple Lane opened for three.

The candidate was vetted per the standing rigor: the plant-house caregiver's own grown daughter, known quantity, references shared at the bench, the liturgy transcribed to two pages and rehearsed at a paid practice hour the Tuesday prior (the page endorses the practice hour without reservation: the sitter runs bath-book-song once with a parent home and invisible, and Friday's unknowns drop by half).

Friday ran as Fridays of this species run. The three-beat goodbye performed at the sitter's arrival, the wail brief, the window post assumed, the car pulling out under the weight of two adults resisting the urge to circle the block. The restaurant: the good one, the tablecloth one, no corner booth required, no fuse to race. And the discoveries, in order: that they had forgotten how long dinner takes when nobody is managing a tray; that the first twenty minutes ran on the old no-baby-talk rule's rails and then leapt them joyfully (the rule, both agreed, existed to be broken by choice rather than default); that the phone, face down per doctrine, could hold one incoming photo, the sitter's thumbs-up beside a crib-bound, starfished, long-asleep subject, and that one photo was enough, was in fact the entire nervous system's price for the evening's second glass.

They were home by ten to a silent house, a read-through of the sitter's notes (bath: splendid; book: four readings; song: performed from the transcription, wrong notes preserved as written), and the old pilgrimage to the crib rail, performed in stocking feet, to watch the breathing of somebody who had needed them zero times in four hours.

The page's counsel, filed under the marriage's maintenance schedule alongside the kitchen dances: the night out is not a luxury purchased from the family's account. It is a deposit into it. The two people who came home had been out with each other, and the household they reentered ran the whole next week on the difference.

Second Friday: booked before bed, while the courage held.

The sitter kept the transcription. The wrong notes, she said, were the best part. She was, all parties agreed, going to work out fine.

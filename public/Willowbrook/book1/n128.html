Night 128

The Texture Tour

Month 5: Nearly Sitting, Nearly Everything

The tour had no tickets and one stop after another, and it ran whenever the weather agreed.

Tree bark, first, the old oak's hide, ridged and patient, Olivia's palm pressed flat to it and her face going through the full committee review: surprise, study, verdict. Then grass, that impossible substance, ten thousand cool blades at once against the back of a hand. A smooth stone from the path's edge, sun-warm. The park bench's weathered plank. Mama's linen sleeve; the stroller's cool frame; on the way home, the front door's brass knob, polished by a century of Willowbrook hands.

The fifth month's hands were ready for the tour in a way the fourth's had not been: reaching accurately, gripping deliberately, and above all reporting, every texture filed by the busiest sensory department in her whole administration. Skin is the largest organ and, this year, the primary textbook. Rough, smooth, cool, warm, yielding, unyielding: each a word she would learn by hand years before mouth, the vocabulary of surfaces underwriting the vocabulary of speech.

The tour's rules were the standing ones, nothing sharp, nothing that fits through the toilet tube, hands washed after the outdoor galleries, plus one worth writing down: narrate the stops. "Rough. That's rough bark." Word to hand, hand to world, the labels applied at the moment of contact, which is when labels stick.

Final stop, at home: the bear's fur, appraised with new expertise, found superior to all bark.

The curator yawned. Museum hours were over.

The collection, as ever, would reopen at dawn, the world being the one exhibit that never travels.

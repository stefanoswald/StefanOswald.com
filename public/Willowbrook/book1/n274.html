Night 274

The Word-a-Week Ledger

Month 10: High Summer

The chalkboard, nap architecture's old registry, semi-retired since the two-nap treaty trued, reopened at the month's start under new management: the word ledger, the vocabulary's trickle made visible on the kitchen wall, one chalked line per minting.

The summer column, as the month ran it. Duck, the founder, grand-mothered in with its Tuesday's date. Ball, dog, more, per the spring's file. Then the season's own coinage, chalked at first certified spend: wawa (the water's own word, minted at the wading tub, spent thereafter on the brook, the bath, the rain, and once, the market issuing its gentle correction, the milk). Baba, ruled after committee review to be the bear's own name (the dresser pointed at each dusk, the coin aimed and repeated; the bear, the almanac notes, took the honor with his usual composure). Up, the first verb-adjacent instrument, deployed with both arms raised at any desired altitude change, a word that was also its own gesture per the era's efficient economies. And nana, the banana's own title, minted at the fourth meal and spent at every yellow sighting in the produce aisle at volumes the market found difficult to decline.

Eight coins chalked by the month's end, the trickle running per the charts' own word-a-week arithmetic, and the page frames the ledger's true accounting for the households chalking their own walls. The spoken column is the visible tenth: beneath each minted word ran the warehouse's whole compounding inventory, the understood vocabulary now measuring in the many dozens per the naming market's year of stocking, the daily proofs mounting (bring the ball; where's Papa's shoe; time for bath, each instruction landing and executing at rates that made the household's speech, both parents noticed, newly consequential: the era of spelling things out loud beginning, per every family's comic tradition, with the word b-a-t-h). And the ledger's purpose, the page notes, was never the counting, the charts' ranges running so wide at this age that the tally proves nothing the checkup didn't, but the seeing: the trickle, chalked, visible, celebrated at each minting per the market's oldest law: you said something, it mattered, someone wrote it on the wall.

"Nana," invoiced the ledger's own author at breakfast, at the fruit bowl, with the point's full authority.

Honored, at market rates. Chalked, the page notes, twice, the author, lifted to the board per petition, adding beneath the state's entry her own line of authenticating scribble: the ledger's first co-signature.

The column ran on all summer, coin by chalked coin, toward the explosion the toddler book's own pages held in inventory.

The wall, the almanac ruled, had never carried better accounts.

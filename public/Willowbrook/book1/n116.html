Night 116

The Massage Minute

Month 4 — Rolling and Laughing

Between the bath and the pajamas, the family had installed a minute nobody wanted to skip.

The towel spread on the nursery rug. A drop of plain baby oil warmed between Mama's palms. And then the old craft, older than every book about it: legs first, long slow strokes thigh to toes, wringing gently like bread dough that giggles. The soles of the feet, thumbed in circles. The belly, clockwise, the direction dinner travels. Arms, shoulder to fingers, each finger uncurled and named at no charge. The back, last, in long strokes that ran her whole length while she lay draped over a thigh like a small satisfied ferry passenger.

Infant massage carries a respectable file of findings — circulation, digestion, deeper sleep, the works — but its realest export is simpler: twenty unhurried touches that say, in the skin's first language, you are safe, you are home, the day is closing gently. Touch is the oldest information there is. A minute of it, nightly, compounds like interest.

The craft's few rules rode along: firm enough not to tickle, soft enough to be weather; stop when she says stop, in the body's grammar; skip the oil near hands that live in mouths.

Somewhere between the second leg and the belly, Olivia went loose all over — the full surrender, limbs at anchor, eyes at half-mast.

Pajamas received a boneless diplomat. The crib received a solved problem.

One minute. Nightly. The best labor rate in the house.

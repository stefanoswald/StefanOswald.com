Night 78

The Comparison Trap

Month 3 — Rhythm

The phone said a baby in another town could roll both ways at nine weeks. The phone showed a nursery with color-coordinated bins and a mother with finished hair. The phone reported, at 11 p.m., that some babies Olivia's age were sleeping twelve hours, eating kale, possibly filing taxes.

Mama put the phone down, and this page is about that motion.

Babies are not a race, but the internet is a racetrack, and every parent wanders onto it some tired night. So here are the rails to hold. Milestones are windows, not deadlines — rolling spans months of normal, first words span a year, walking wider still, and the late-walking baby becomes the same eight-year-old as the early one. Highlight reels are not days; nobody posts the 4 p.m. crying, theirs or the baby's. And the only chart that matters hangs at the doctor's office, where Olivia's own curve climbed her own hill at her own correct speed.

The real baby, meanwhile, was on the rug, gnawing the wooden ring with visible genius.

The test worth taking is the one Mama took then: eyes off the screen, onto the child. Content? Curious? Herself? All boxes, checked.

"You're not ahead or behind," she told her. "You're exactly Olivia."

It was, and remains, the whole entire truth. The phone slept face down, uncomforted. Everyone else slept fine.

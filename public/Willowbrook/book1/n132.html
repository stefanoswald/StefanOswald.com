Night 132

The Washcloth in the Icebox

Month 5: Nearly Sitting, Nearly Everything

The fussing had a new flavor this week, chewier, more gum-forward, and the icebox held the answer, folded in quarters.

The washcloth trick had come down from Grandpa Joe, as most icebox tricks do: one clean cloth, one corner dampened, an hour in the cold, and then offered like a delicacy. Olivia seized it and went to work, gumming the chilled corner with the sigh of a traveler reaching an inn.

The fifth month lives in teething's long foothills, real summits still ahead, the first white crowns commonly cresting around six or seven months, some babies sooner, plenty later, all of them on schedule by their own almanac. The foothills have their weather: gums that itch and swell faintly, the chew imperative at full mandate, drool at spring volumes, and evenings when everything is somehow wrong by one degree.

The relief kit stayed the honest classics. Cold, never frozen, true ice burns small mouths. Pressure: the clean parental knuckle, rubbed firm along the ridge, a service received tonight with closed eyes, like a spa client of long standing. Texture, bumps and nubs outperforming smooth. And the patience to re-offer, re-chill, repeat.

The almanac's fine print rode along: fever, true fever, is never teething's doing, that rumor sends real illness unexamined to bed. The thermometer keeps its own books, and the doctor's line stays open for them.

The corner warmed; the cloth returned to the cold for its next shift.

Olivia, unclenched by one full degree, accepted the evening after all.

In the icebox, the relief waited, folded, patient, comfort at forty degrees, the oldest prescription in the drawer.

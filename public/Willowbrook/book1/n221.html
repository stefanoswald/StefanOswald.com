Night 221

Uh-Oh

Month 8: The Velcro Weeks and the Thaw

The Dropping Game, that venerable institution, founded in the fourth month, renamed by the doctrine of the sixth, staffed throughout by one large unpaid assistant, acquired in the eighth its official anthem, and the anthem was two syllables long.

"Uh-oh."

It had been modeled at her, of course, per the universal parental reflex, at every spill and topple since the feast began: uh-oh, the small ceremonial noise the tribe makes when gravity wins. This month she took delivery. First as echo, the spoon down, the parental uh-oh, her syllables trailing after like a small cover version. Then, within the week, the upgrade that changed the game's entire dramaturgy: the uh-oh moved before the drop.

Spoon lifted. Extended overside. Eye contact achieved and held, the theater's rules being absolute on this point. And then, with the relish of a diva at the aria: "Uh-oh." Release. Clatter. House down.

The page unpacks what the two syllables carry, because they carry the eighth month entire. A word, arguably her third, after the aimed ma-ma and da-da, and her first abstraction: not a name for a person but a label for an event, deployed accurately at every fall, spill, and collapse in her jurisdiction (and, by month's end, at Papa's dropped keys, the generalization the language texts wait for, the word traveling to new cases on its own). A joke, the pre-announcement converting accident's vocabulary into comedy's, the timing structure of the authored peekaboo now running on language. And a flag of the era's deepest work: she knew what was about to happen, said so, and made it happen, prediction, announcement, execution, the whole grammar of intention in one soggy transaction.

The assistant's contract, the page confirms, remained in force per its sixth-month amendments: three retrievals with full theatrical sincerity, then the tether toy's shift. But the assistant would confess, off the record, that the era's retrievals were underpriced at any wage: the eye contact, the held beat, the uh-oh delivered like news from the front...

Some tickets, the almanac noted, you cannot buy. You can only be on shift when the show runs.

"Uh-oh," said the whole house now, at everything, all month, in her exact intonation.

It was the correct response to most of it. It usually is.

Night 129

The Yes Space

Month 5 — Nearly Sitting, Nearly Everything

The fifth month's rolling had range now, and the house made its answer: a country where every law was yes.

One corner of the living room, gated wide, and inside it — nothing. Nothing dangerous, which took an afternoon of knee-level auditing; and nearly nothing at all, which took the discipline the toy rotation had already taught. The rug, cleared. Three toys, on rotation. The low mirror on its wall. Room, in every direction, to roll.

The yes space, the philosophy calls it, and the philosophy has the goods: a baby in a space with no no's needs no hovering, no snatching-away, no chorus of corrections — and a baby unhovered plays deeper, tries harder things, and learns the oldest lesson of good rooms, which is that the world can be trusted where it has been made trustworthy. The parent, meanwhile, gains the thing the fifth month sells nowhere else: minutes. Kettle minutes, bathroom minutes, minutes of sitting down entire.

The audit's checklist earned its page: outlets capped, cords vanished, the bookcase bolted to its wall (furniture tips; anchor everything, everywhere, this year, not later), the floor swept at pebble resolution, the gate latched against the wider world's opinions.

Olivia was deposited in her new republic and rolled its full breadth immediately, twice, a landowner pacing the property.

Then she lay in its middle, working the ring, wholly unsupervised and wholly safe — the two conditions the whole architecture existed to make true at once.

From the kitchen, over a hot and finished cup of tea, the government observed its citizen flourishing under liberty.

Yes, said the space, to everything in it. It was the entire legal code, and it held.

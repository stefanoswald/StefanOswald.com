Night 99

Ba

Month 4 — Rolling and Laughing

For three months, the conversation had run on vowels — the coos, the ah-goos, the long open notes of the original language.

Then one afternoon, over nothing at all, Olivia pressed her lips together, gathered steam, and issued a "ba."

Just that. Ba. But Mama froze like she had heard the town bell ring underwater, because that small sound had a hinge in it: a consonant. Lips meeting, stopping air, releasing it on purpose — the first hard edge in the river of vowels, and the very stuff words will someday be cut from.

"Ba!" said Mama back, delighted.

"Ba," Olivia confirmed. Then, expansively: "Ba-ba-ba."

The babbling season opens like this in the fourth and fifth months — b's and m's and p's first, the lip letters, the ones a baby can watch being made on every face she loves. The answering game is the whole curriculum: echo hers back, offer one nearby ("ma!"), pause for the return serve. Every round wires the loop between ear and mouth that speech will run on for life.

And a note for the tender-hearted, posted early: when "ma-ma" and "da-da" arrive in the babble — soon — they will be sounds first, names later. She will call the lamp da-da for a while. The lamp will not deserve it. Patience; the aim improves.

"Ba," said Olivia to the lamp, to the bear, to her foot, all evening.

Everything was ba. It was a ba kind of world, and she had the word for it.

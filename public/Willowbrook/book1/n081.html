Night 81

The Phone Goes Face Down

Month 3 — Rhythm

Feeds were long, and the phone was always right there, and thumbs get hungry too.

Nobody could fault it — the 2 a.m. scroll was survival some nights, company in the loneliest chair in the house, and this page is not here to shame that. But somewhere mid-month, Mama noticed the pattern: Olivia's eyes, during feeds, searching upward. Finding the underside of a phone. Waiting. And she remembered the eight inches — feeding distance, the exact focal length of new eyes, the original classroom where faces are learned.

So the house adopted the gentlest possible rule: one feed a day, minimum, phones face down, eyes available. The dusk feed became the appointed one — no scroll, no screen, just the ancient two-way stare that neuroscientists measure in papers and parents measure in the chest.

What happens in that stare has a boring name — attunement — and an unboring effect: her gaze catches yours, yours answers, hers deepens, serve and return with the eyes alone. It is the first conversation, older than words, and it only requires attendance.

Some feeds stayed scroll feeds. Parents are humans; 2 a.m. is 2 a.m.

But the dusk feed belonged to the stare now, both parties present.

"Hello," said Mama's eyes.

"Hello hello hello," said Olivia's, which had no volume control yet.

Night 105

Grandparents' Day

Month 4 — Rolling and Laughing

There is a particular physics to grandparents, and the fourth month is when families first measure it properly.

At Honeywell Farm, Grandpa Joe held Olivia with the terrible ease of a man who had done forty harvests and two babies, and the kitchen table wore more food than three adults could address. This was law. Grandparent houses produce food in proportion to love, and the love was considerable.

The visit ran on the treaty every family negotiates, mostly silently. The grandparent clause: he got to spoil her — the extra holding, the wrong songs, the flagrant narration of her perfections. Some rules bend at the farm; that is what farms are for. The parent clause, non-negotiable and gently held: the safety floor travels intact. Back to sleep, even here. The car seat rules, even for the quarter mile to see the neighbor's new foal. The nap, defended even against a grandfather's assertion that "she doesn't look tired," delivered while she visibly dissolved.

He grumbled the ceremonial grumble and complied entire, because that is the secret fourth clause: grandparents test the fence exactly once, to confirm the parents will hold it, and respect nothing more than when they do.

She napped in the farmhouse crib, the one that had held her father.

The grown-ups drank coffee in the kitchen, three generations arranged around one sleeping fourth, saying not much, meaning all of it.

"Bring her Sundays," said Grandpa Joe at the gate, as if it hadn't already been decided by everyone, including the chickens.

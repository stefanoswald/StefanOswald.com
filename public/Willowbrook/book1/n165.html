Night 165

Follow the Leader

Month 6 — The Feast Begins

With citizenship came the republic's first sport, invented the way all the great ones are: by accident, at dusk, in a hallway.

Papa, retrieving a sock at floor level, found himself regarded from four feet away by a crawler with narrowed eyes and a rising grin — and some old instinct made him thump the floor once and scuttle backward. The effect was total. She shrieked the hunting shriek and came on at flank speed. He fled at quarter-crawl around the couch; she cut the corner like a veteran; he was caught at the bookshelf, boarded, and summited, and the hallway rang like a playground.

Follow the leader. Chase. The game with no equipment and no age of expiry, opening now its earliest edition, and the page files its quiet credentials beneath the noise.

The chase is the first game of together-motion — my move answers your move — turn-taking at the speed of joy, the serve-and-return of the babble months gone gross-motor. It drills the new crawl under the finest possible conditions: motivation total, terrain known, falls onto carpet and pride only. And the being-chased half teaches its own deep lesson, the delicious safe-danger of pursuit by a monster who is also the safest person alive — fear's play-edition, inoculating in tiny doses, which is what much of play turns out to be.

House rules, brief: floors clear per the standing audit; the monster loses often and dramatically; the catch ends in tickles or triumph, never startle; and the game retires at the first overtired shriek-change, joy's currency being counterfeit past that line.

Three rounds ran that night. The monster was defeated in all of them, comprehensively, collapsing at last in the hallway while the victor pounded his shoulder blades like a small drummer accepting a title.

Bath ran late. The almanac logged the sport's founding anyway, dated, two handwritings, one still laughing.

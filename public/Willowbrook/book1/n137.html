Night 137

What Rosa Knows

Month 5 — Nearly Sitting, Nearly Everything

Some pages record what the village teaches. This one records an afternoon's syllabus, porch edition, both babies asleep and the coffee gone honest.

Rosa had been at this eleven days longer, plus one older niece's worth of apprenticeship, and she dispensed her holdings the way the best village women do — unranked, unpushed, offered like plates at a potluck.

That the four-month regression she'd feared for Lily had come and gone in twelve days, and the fearing had cost more than the regression. That her own mother's advice ran thirty percent gold and seventy percent era, and the sorting, once she stopped resenting it, was easy: safety from the doctors, soothing from the grandmothers, sanity from the bench. That freezer dinners assembled in one Sunday hour had saved more evenings than any philosophy. That the guilt kept office hours at her house too — hers convened in the shower — and that saying it out loud to another mother cut its docket in half.

And the one Mama carried home, turning it over like a river stone: "Nobody's doing it right. Everybody's doing it over. The ones who look finished are just further from their last hard week, or closer to their next one."

The babies slept through the entire lecture series, tuition-free.

The page's counsel is only this: find your Rosa. Then — the half forgotten — be one. The porch has two chairs for a reason, and the syllabus only exists spoken.

The strollers stirred at five. The university adjourned, sine die, till Tuesday.

Night 244

The First Step

Month 9 — The Edge of Everything

It came on a Wednesday, before lunch, without announcement, the way every page of the almanac had promised it would and no page had made believable.

The setting, for the record the family would keep forever: the living room interior, late morning, the wagon docked, the stander at the ten-second trade mid-floor per the new custom — and Mama, three feet off, crouched with arms open, running the old game that had produced nothing all week: come here, come to me, one step, come on.

And the weight shifted. The right foot — the almanac records it was the right — unloaded, lifted, swung, and landed, half a small stride forward, the whole apparatus lurching after it into Mama's catch.

One step. The step.

The house did not stay calm, whatever the doctrine advises about weather during miracles. The catch became a swing became a shout that brought Papa from the kitchen at a run; the video call went out to two addresses inside the hour (the farm's "Well now" arriving at a register the family had never documented; Rosa's whole kitchen audibly cheering down the wire); the almanac's entry was boxed, double-boxed, dated to the minute, and signed — the page notes this is the ledger's only signed entry — by both hands.

The page files the milestone's true print beneath the celebration, for every household at their own three feet of open floor. That the first step is one step: the second came four days later, the third with it, and the count then held at three-and-a-catch for most of a week — walking arriving not as a switch but as a bargain, the crawl retaining the freight routes for weeks yet while the new gait ran its short prestige service, three steps, five, a lurching seven, always ending in arms or floor and always, this is the age's grace, retried. That the timing — here at the ninth month's opening, per this baby's own almanac — sits early in the great nine-to-eighteen-month range the book has posted since the fireflies, and buys, per the oldest counsel, nothing but itself: the walkers of twelve and seventeen months arrive at the same kindergarten on the same legs. And that the catch matters as much as the step: the arms at three feet, the falls received as landings, the entire enterprise conducted per the frustration-fuel doctrine at the edge of ability where all the year's learning has lived.

That night the liturgy ran as ever — bath, book, song, the crib — over a citizen who had, since breakfast, changed species.

"She walked," said Papa at the rail, trying the sentence's fit.

"Maybe," said Mama — the old word, one last time, at the old threshold; then, quietly, retiring it for this one case only, because some facts close even that word's accounts:

"She walked."

Outside, the peepers sang it down to the brook, and the brook, which knows everything first, carried it to the willow before midnight, per the town's own liturgy.

One step. The whole rest of the road, hers now.

Goodnight, walker.

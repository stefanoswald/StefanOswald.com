Night 86

The Overnight Bag Question

Month 3 — Rhythm

Grandpa Joe made the offer at Sunday dinner, plain as bread: "Leave her the night sometime. I raised two on this farm and lost neither."

The silence that followed contained one entire marriage's worth of conversation, conducted by eyebrow.

The overnight question comes to every family on its own schedule, and this page declines to rule on the timing — some parents are ready at three months, some at three years, and the baby, honestly, is the flexible one. What it will say is what the night eventually taught them.

They said not yet, that Sunday. Then, two Sundays on, they said yes to the trial size: an evening, not a night. Bag packed with the checklist taped in the lid — feeding notes, the bedtime liturgy written out step by step, the doctor's number Grandpa Joe accepted solemnly and never glanced at.

He did it all differently, of course. Sang wrong songs, righter than he knew. Walked her along the fence line to say goodnight to the chickens, an item appearing nowhere in the liturgy.

She slept like hay in a barn.

And the parents, three hours alone in a restaurant with appetizers, spent the first hour talking about her and the second remembering how to talk — both hours necessary, in that order.

"Anytime," said Grandpa Joe at pickup, handing back the sleeping parcel.

The word sat in the bank after that, gathering its quiet interest: anytime. Some nights, just knowing it is there is the whole rest.

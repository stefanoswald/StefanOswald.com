Night 281

The Animal Orchestra

Month 10 — High Summer

The naming market opened, mid-month, its loudest counter: the animal sounds — the moo and the woof and the quack arriving to the ledger not as words exactly but as the era's own hybrid coinage, and trading, the page certifies at once, at full currency.

The recital, as the nightly book convened it. The barnyard spread per the pointing liturgy — jab, at the cow. And the market's standard quote ("cow") now preceded, by the customer's own new custom, with the sound's own bid: "Moo." Jab, the dog: "Woof" (rendered per the household phonology as "fuff," the almanac preserving the edition). Jab, the duck — the founder's own portrait: "Kack kack," the coin's sound-cousin minted beside it. The recital running the spread's whole cast nightly now, the reader demoted per the era's happy custom to accompanist: the sounds hers, the names his, the orchestra two-voiced at every stand.

The page files the sound-words' linguistics at their true rank, because the moo era is no detour. The animal sounds are the vocabulary's perfect on-ramp — acoustically vivid, emotionally charged, endlessly rehearsed at market (the book's cast, the farm's fence lines, the lane's own gold dog supplying live confirmation at every walk) — and the charts count them as words entire: the moo aimed at the cow's portrait carries the full semantic freight, sound soldered to referent per the beam's oldest law. The counter's deeper trade: the sounds are the child's first phonological playground — the vowels stretched (the moo running four seconds at the recital's enthusiasms), the consonants drilled (the k of the kack the mouth's newest hard edge) — the animal orchestra running, under its comedy, the speech apparatus's own summer conservatory.

And the live recitals, logged at the month's rounds: the farm's Sunday running the full program at the fence lines (Henrietta quoted her own bid at point-blank; the Duchess, situation permitting, declined comment per policy), the pond's eleven confirming the kack in chorus per their spring custom, and the lane's gold dog — the year's oldest acquaintance — receiving his "fuff" at the fence with the tail's own metronome answering, the two of them trading their species' greetings per a diplomacy the whole town had watched compile since the stroller's first pass.

"Moo," offered the orchestra at lights-out, at the ceiling, the day's last program note.

"Moo," confirmed the accompanist, in the settling register, per the era's whole two-voiced custom.

The barnyard slept. The counter held its morning hours. The coinage, sound and name in tandem, compounded toward the sentences the toddler book held in inventory — the moo of this summer, the page notes, being the same instrument that would someday read the whole spread aloud to a smaller listener, per the way these orchestras always, always tour.

Night 277

The Tomato

Month 10 — High Summer

The appointment, chalked in the almanac since the planting shift — August, the stake's row, verdict pending — came due at the month's turning: the garden bureau's tomato row ran red at last, and the harvest lesson convened.

The ceremony, per the season's own liturgy. The morning round arrived at the row per the standing docket — the stake's inspection, run daily since the planting at the gardener's own insistence, delivering this morning the finding the whole summer had staked: color. The first cherry tomato at full August red, sun-warm at the vine's low margin, at exactly the certified pincer's own altitude.

The pick: hers — the page stipulates the ceremony's whole constitution rode on the pronoun — the stem shown once by the senior partner's fingers (the gentle-twist craft, the summit's one-finger heritage running now at harvest grade), then the small hand closing per its year of training, the twist, the give, and the tomato arriving whole into the palm that had watered its row since the seedling's four-handed installation.

The regard: the builder's gravity, garden edition — the fruit turned in the grip, examined at the committee's full term, the lookback firing at the senior partner not for confirmation but, the almanac swears it, for witness: see what the row made. See what we made.

And the eating: in the row, standing, per the oldest lesson no market teaches — the tomato bitten at the customs office's full ceremony, the sun-warmth arriving off the vine at temperatures no kitchen ever shipped, the verdict running the face's whole gallery and landing, emphatically, in the Rapture Wing's permanent collection: the eyes closing, the free hand slapping the thigh per the era's highest ratings, and the petition fired at the vine itself before the first was swallowed: more.

More, honored the row, at the vine's own rates, all month.

The page banks the harvest's whole syllabus, per the planting shift's forecast. The food chain, walked at first person: the seed, the water, the wait, the fruit — the summer's longest cause-and-effect, run at the season's own pace and closed in the row's own gold, the lesson the pouches and the produce aisles spend the raising years quietly unteaching. The patience curriculum, paid at term: the row inspected eighty mornings for this one, the waiting itself — the page notes per the whole book's economy — the tuition. And the bureau's deepest wage, entered in the senior hand at the day's file: she will not remember this tomato. The tomato does not mind. The remembering was never the crop. The growing was. Both grew.

"Nana?" petitioned the gardener at the row's edge, hopeful, the categories trading wide per custom.

"Tomato," corrected the market, laughing. "Mato," minted the ledger on the spot — coin the ninth, chalked that noon with the harvest's own date.

The vine ran red through September. The gardener, certified at harvest grade, picked the row's whole term.

First crop: one tomato, eaten standing. Yield, per the almanac's accounting: total.

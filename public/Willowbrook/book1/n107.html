Night 107

The Low Voice

Month 4 — Rolling and Laughing

Papa had one instrument nobody else in the house could play: the rumble.

It lived below the words, down in the sternum, and Olivia had known it longer than she had known air — deep voices carry through walls and water, and his had reached her for months before she was born, the bass line under Mama's melody. Now, fussy nights, the old frequency still worked like a key. Ear to his chest, his voice dropped low and slow — reading anything, the news, the seed catalog — and her whole small system down-shifted, gear by gear, breathing lengthening to match the tide of it.

The science tips its hat here: low-frequency sound settles infants measurably, and fathers arrive factory-equipped. But the page's true business is the encouragement, because fourth months are when some Papas quietly conclude they hold second chair — the milk is elsewhere, the soothing defaults elsewhere, the baby cranes past them at witching hour toward headquarters.

Second chair is a rumor. The instrument proves otherwise. The rumble, the airplane arms, the unhurried evening walk on shoulders that never rush — a father's kit is different, not lesser, and babies build different rooms for each parent, all of them load-bearing.

Tonight the seed catalog, read in cello register, dropped her off by page four.

"Turnips," rumbled Papa one last time, to be sure.

Gone. The instrument rested. First chair, in its section — and the section, hers alone.

Night 273

The Crickets Take the Ledger

Month 9 — The Edge of Everything

The peepers' spring contract ran out with the solstice, per the brook's own labor calendar, and the summer night watch posted its veteran shift: the crickets — the ledger-keepers, the town's own abacus, running the warm dark's accounts from every hedge and garden border at the rate the season set.

Stella came down through the deep June air for the month's handover, the willow's hill gone full green below her, and the Sleepy Moon leaned in at its summer angle to hum the receiving hum against the counting.

"The ninth chapter's closed," Stella reported, over the ledger's run. "And it was the one, you know. The one the whole file was building toward." She ticked the summary per her office's spare style: "She walked. Wednesday before lunch, three feet, into the catch. Talks now — duck first; the pond's eleven confirmed it in chorus; I heard them from altitude. Met a worm gently. Built a tower. Stood in her first storm at the window and filed the thunder under magnificent — they held the face, that family; they held it the whole year." A pause, the star's own ledger running. "And Bella's back. Landed on the child's own tomato stake, last day of the month. Thirty seconds, unbrokered. The town does not miss its appointments."

"Mmmmm," hummed the Moon — which is Moon for no, it never has, not once in all my shifts; and for the other thing, the one the whole sky's watch had been carrying since the melt: say the number.

"Ten weeks," said Stella. "Minus one. The candle's country."

The crickets took the number into the ledger and ran it down the lane's whole length, hedge to hedge, per the town's own liturgy — past the porch swings and the bench's parliament, down to the brook (which knew first, per its office, and had known all along), up the hill to the willow (which let its long green down over the news the way it does when the year is being weighed), and out the fence lines to the far pasture where a red balloon rode at anchor above the summer dark, keeping its own account of Sundays.

Below, on Maple Lane, the small correct darkness held at its window: the crib, the bear at his dresser watch with one flopped ear, the envelope in the keepsake harbor with its soft cargo, the walker asleep at four words and three weeks' mileage — the whole ninth chapter folded down under the blackout's taped edge, the tenth already loading in the gold-edged dark.

Stella twinkled once, for yes, and settled to the summer watch.

Nine months down in the town of Willowbrook. The crickets counting. The candle's country, ten weeks wide, opening below.

Goodnight, walker.

Goodnight, Willowbrook.

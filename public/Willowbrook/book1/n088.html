Night 88

What the Hands Know Now

Month 3 — Rhythm

Take the quarterly inventory of two small hands.

They open in advance now, shaped to the size of the thing approached — wide for the cloth block, narrower for the ring, a fact so ordinary no one notices it and so intricate that engineers weep. They steer: left hand to the left country, right to the right, and lately across the middle to raid each other's holdings, that border crossing the therapists watch for with quiet delight.

They deliver everything to the mouth, laboratory of record, and — newest clause — they let go on purpose sometimes, releasing the block to the floor and observing the result with the cool interest of a physicist funding gravity research.

They know Mama's hair (grip: profound). They know Papa's beard, the burp cloth's hem, which corner of the block has the crinkle in it. They keep office hours during every feed, one hand resting on the warm bottle or opening and closing slowly against skin, filing tactile reports throughout the meal.

Three months ago they were weather — waving, random, startled by their own passing. Now they are staff.

The parental assignment continues unchanged and unglamorous: floor time, reachable objects, no rescuing the fumbles. The fumbles are the tuition.

Tonight the left hand held the block while the right one patted it, twice, gently — approval, or possibly a memo.

The committee of two adjourned at bath time, unwashed opinions and all.

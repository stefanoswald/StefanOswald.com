Night 256

The Chick Brigade

Month 9: The Edge of Everything

The Sunday liturgy arrived, mid-month, at the farm's own spring headline: the brooder box in the barn's warm corner, and inside it, new that week, the chick brigade. Eleven of them, gold and peeping, the Duchess's own late clutch (her situation, Grandpa Joe reported, having resolved into motherhood, which surprised the whole coop and the Duchess most of all).

The introduction, staged by the farm's senior diplomat with forty springs of such stagings in his hands. The approach: the deputy on his knee at the box's rim, the brigade's peeping received first at watching range per the doctrine's eternal first clause, the voltage managed, the pointing arm conducting the entire eleven, the new word attempted ("duh!" corrected at market, "chick; peep peep," the coin banked visibly for later minting). The presentation: one chick lifted in the cupped scarred hands to her eye level, the gold astonishment of it at eight inches, the peeping at first-person volume, the tiny machinery of it studied by the full committee in the barn's lamplight. And the touch: the one-finger protocol, freshly certified at the worm summit and administered now under four generations of supervision (the hands that had taught her father's father's fingers this same stroke, the page notes, holding the curriculum steady across the century), one gentle stroke, crown to back, the softness arriving off the fingertip visibly, the second stroke self-petitioned and softer, per the summit's own precedent.

"Gentle," confirmed the diplomat, in the register the fence lines had taught all year.

Gentle, held the finger. The chick, unbothered, peeped its own minutes. The brigade below peeped the chorus. The deputy's face ran the year's full parade and landed on its newest expression, the tenderness, the almanac names it plainly, first documented edition: the voltage not of wonder or of triumph but of care, aimed at something smaller than herself.

The page banks the farm's spring syllabus entire. The baby animals of the season, the chicks, the neighbor's April lambs viewed at the fence, the pond's own ducklings convoying behind their admiral (named, of course, at full spend: "duck! duck! duck!"), meeting the child at the year's perfect window: old enough for the gentle-finger's treaty, young enough that the smallness of things is still her own country, the care curriculum enrolling its student at peer scale. And the farm's deeper standing lesson, running under every Sunday since the first fence-line introductions: the world keeps making new things, and the new things are handed carefully, and the hands that hold them learned it from other hands, the whole town's oldest liturgy, conducted in a barn corner at eight inches, peeping.

The brigade was visited twice more before the drive home, the finger gentle at every audience.

"Peep," issued the deputy at the barn door, departing, the coin minted after all, aimed back at the box, first spend.

"Well now," said Grandpa Joe, at his fullest documented register since the steps.

The barn held its gold peeping lamplight behind them, eleven strong, growing.

Sundays, said everything, as ever. The farm's spring, banked whole.

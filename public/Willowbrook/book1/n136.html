Night 136

The Second Summer Walk

Month 5 — Nearly Sitting, Nearly Everything

The evening walk had been a soothing measure once — motion for the witching hour, scenery as sedative. The fifth month rewrote its purpose entirely.

She rode upright now, canopy back, strapped tall in the stroller's new recline, and the walk was hers. The world came to the wheels and she received it: the neighbor's cat, tracked across a lawn with full head-swivel. The porch waves, answered lately with an arm-pump that stopped traffic. The string lights coming on down Maple Lane, greeted each evening as breaking news of the best kind.

Papa narrated the town at strolling pace — mailbox, maple, moon coming up early — and the town narrated back. Willowbrook at dusk was a curriculum: dogs at exactly her new fascination level, the bakery's day-end smell, the brook heard under its small bridge, every porch a flashcard. The stroller had become a lecture hall doing four miles an hour through the syllabus.

The walk earned its page for what it gave each species aboard. For her: the day's last course of world, served at digestible speed, and the settling that motion still worked on the far side of it. For him: the presence the walk enforced — no screens at stroller handles, the town too interesting on both sides of the conversation.

They took the long way past the park, by referendum of one pumping arm.

Home at full dark, one baby world-fed and heavy-lidded, one father lighter than he had left.

The bath was running as they came in. The string lights held the lane behind them, on watch till morning.

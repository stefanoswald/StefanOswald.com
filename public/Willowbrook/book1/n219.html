Night 219

Three Meals, Standing Rhythm

Month 8: The Velcro Weeks and the Thaw

Somewhere in the eighth month, without a treaty signing, the feast's scattered engagements consolidated into government: three meals, at the family's own hours, load-bearing.

Breakfast, post-dawn-shift, the Cheerio trials and a soft scramble, the day's first sitting. Lunch, before the anchor nap, the plant-house running its own parallel service on daycare days per the shared liturgy. Dinner, the original theater, at the table with the state, the family menu adapted trayward per the soup-weather doctrine, salt held, shapes legal, spices enrolled.

And around the three pillars, the milk, still the nutrition's senior partner at this age, the page notes against the era's common miscount, but restructured: the grazing feeds of the early months consolidated to a working four, waking, mid-morning, post-nap, bedtime, fitted between meals like mortar between stones, no longer the stones themselves.

The rhythm's arrival changed more than the menu. Appetite, given architecture, became legible: hungry at the pillars, genuinely, the meals arriving to a customer rather than a negotiation. The strange mid-afternoon fusses of the seventh month stood revealed in hindsight as scheduling debt, paid off by the pillar system. And the family meal, the institution this book has built toward since the bouncer joined the table in month three, now ran three times daily with a full member seated: eating what the table ate, at the pace the table set, in the conversation's weather, her name in the sentences, her gavel in the minutes.

The page files the standing counsel for the rhythm's keeping. The pillars at consistent hours, the body's clock being the appetite's best friend. The division of labor, the feeding literature's oldest law, now fully in force: the parents govern the what and the when; she governs the whether and the how much, the plate offered, the appetite sovereign, no siege, no dessert diplomacy, the trust in her own full-o-meter maintained as the lifelong asset it is. And the meals kept, wherever possible, communal: the food matters; the table matters more.

Dinner that night ran the full architecture: soup's third season, bread gummed at the crust per tradition, one green bean accepted without incident (ballot fourteen; the gallery noted it without fanfare, per curatorial discipline).

Three stones, four mortars, one member in full standing.

The government, fed, governed. The rhythm held the day. The day, held, returned the favor nightly.

Night 147

The Five-Month Stare

Month 5 — Nearly Sitting, Nearly Everything

It happened at the tail of the dusk feed, in the boring room, world muffled, no bells ringing.

Dinner done, drowse not yet arrived, Olivia lay in the crook and looked up — and stayed. Not the scanning look, not the tracking look. The long look: eyes holding eyes, unhurried, both-ways, her one free hand rising to rest on Mama's cheek like a small warm signature.

A minute. More. Neither party filed for adjournment.

The fifth month deepens this — the mutual gaze acquiring duration, the attunement of the early weeks compounding into something the researchers measure with instruments and the parents measure with the sternum. In the held stare, two nervous systems sync their weather: heart rates settling toward each other, the baby's system borrowing the calm of the larger one, the larger one borrowing something back that has no clinical name and needs none.

The page's counsel is only availability. The long stares cannot be scheduled or begun on purpose; they arrive at the margins — feed's end, wake's edge, the pause after the song — and they require exactly one parental skill: not being the one who looks away first. The phone face down. The next task unstarted. The minute permitted to be a minute.

They will thin with the years, these stares, rationed by walking and words and doors closed politely — which is the arithmetic that makes them, right now, the wealth of the age.

The hand patted the cheek once, proprietary. The eyes went, at last, to half-mast.

Mama stayed till the signature slid off, then a while past that, spendthrift with the minute, as the page advises everyone to be.

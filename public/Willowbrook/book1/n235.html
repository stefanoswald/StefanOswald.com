Night 235

Naming the Pictures

Month 8 — The Velcro Weeks and the Thaw

The nightly book, co-operated since the page-turner era and shuffled ever since, transacted this month a new business: the pictures acquired prices, and the reader's finger did the pointing.

The mechanism, run at every sitting now. The page opened — the bear book's meadow spread, say, or the farm book's crowded barnyard — and the small finger descended: jab. On the dog. The face up: expectant, the naming beam thrown wide. "Dog," supplied the market. Jab — the same dog, confirming the quote. "Dog. Big brown dog." Jab — the duck now, new listing. "Duck!" Jab, jab — the market running at the pace demand set, which was considerable, the whole spread priced item by item, some listings requested nightly for weeks (the barnyard's tractor held the month's volume record, unchallenged).

The page holds the finding under the game, and it is the eighth month's largest, running silently beneath all its louder commerce: the receptive vocabulary — the words understood, months ahead of any word spoken — was lighting up like windows at dusk, dozens deep already and compounding nightly. The tests ran themselves, unstaged: Where's the duck? — the finger descending on the duck, four spreads deep in the shuffle. Where's Papa's shoe? at floor level — the crawl redirecting, the shoe achieved and mouthed. Bring the bear — and the bear, astonishingly, arriving, hauled crib-side by one ear per the fetch economy's expanding routes.

Comprehension, the language texts remind every household straining for the first spoken words, is the iceberg; speech is the tip. The child pointing at the named duck holds the word entire — filed, retrievable, aimed — lacking only the mouth's machinery, which trails the mind's by months as ever, drilling its chains in the wings. The parents' market work — the naming supplied on demand, the beam completed every time, the labels attached at the finger's exact moment per the texture tour's old law — was stocking a warehouse whose doors would open, per the almanac's forecast and every chart's, in the year's second act: the naming explosion of the toddler country, when the warehouse ships everything at once.

Tonight's market closed per custom at the lamp's dimming, final listings priced (moon, at the window, jab; star, jab; the quotes supplied in the reading voice's lowest register), the trader carried to the crib with the day's inventory folding into whatever babies file at the day's end — the windows lighting, one by one, behind the small dark glass.

"Dog," said the market's last quote of the night, unsolicited, from the crib, into the dark.

Both parents froze in the doorway.

Sound or word? The almanac's entry ran one line and hedged nothing:

Close. So close. The warehouse doors are rattling.

Night 173

The Sign for More

Month 6 — The Feast Begins

The hands had been shown the sign for weeks — Mama's fingertips bunched and tapped together at every refill, "more? more" — planted like all seeds, on faith, into apparent silence.

This Tuesday, over sweet potato, the silence answered.

The bowl stood empty. The diner regarded it, regarded Mama, and then — deliberately, wobbling, unmistakably — brought her own two hands together in front of her chest, patted them once, twice, and looked up with the face of someone who has said a thing and expects the world to act on it.

More.

The world acted at speed. The bowl refilled as if by covenant; the signer received her wages and banked, in one transaction, the era's great discovery: I can put a thought in another head, on purpose, and the head will move.

Baby signs earn their page on exactly this arithmetic. The hands run months ahead of the mouth — the wanting and the understanding sit fully formed behind lips that cannot yet ship a word, and the gap is where the season's loudest frustrations live. A handful of signs bridges it: more, all done, milk, the working vocabulary of a high chair's whole economy. The research long ago retired the old worry — signing babies talk on schedule and often sooner, the signs scaffolding speech, not rivaling it — and the method costs nothing: sign spoken words at their moment, daily, on faith, until the silence answers.

All done arrived within the fortnight, flung open-palmed like a tiny umpire. Milk followed by November.

The frustration ledger, the almanac confirms, ran measurably quieter all that winter.

"More," said the hands at breakfast, at the swing, at the tenth reading of the bear book.

More, agreed the world, moving. It was the correct answer nearly every time. It usually is, at that address.

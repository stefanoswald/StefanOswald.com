Night 45

Sing Anyway

Month 2 — Smiles and Sleep

Papa could not sing. This was not modesty; it was documented. At weddings, kind people asked him to hum quieter.

But a strange thing happens when a baby arrives: the audience changes, and this audience had no standards at all. She had exactly one criterion — is it you? — and he met it every time.

So he sang. He sang the burping song, an original composition. He sang the socks song, to the tune of something he had ruined. He performed the evening news in opera. Olivia watched him the way sunflowers watch the sun.

Here is the science smuggled into the silliness: melody is language wearing its brightest clothes. Songs stretch the vowels, repeat the patterns, and glue words to rhythm — which is why babies learn from singing even faster than from speech, and why every culture that has babies has lullabies. Off-key delivers all of it. Pitch is for concert halls; babies grade on warmth.

"He's flat," Mama observed from the doorway.

"She's clapping," Papa countered. She was doing nothing of the sort, having no clapping yet, but her feet were kicking in what any fair court would call rhythm.

The concert ran nightly. Reviews were rapturous. No refunds were ever requested.

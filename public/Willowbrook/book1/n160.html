Night 160

Halfway

Month 6 — The Feast Begins

No cake marked it; halfway is not a party. It is a porch, at dusk, at the end of an ordinary Tuesday, with the season's first cool edge in the air and a baby on a knee and the arithmetic arriving unbidden: six months down. Six to go before the candle.

The page lets the parents sit a while before it speaks.

They had crossed, somewhere in the feast weeks, the strange meridian every first year hides: she had now been outside longer than she was in. The kicks Mama once read through her own skin were public property these days — performed in high chairs, applauded at fence lines. The heartbeat Papa first heard through a monitor in a bright terrible-wonderful room now thumped against his collarbone nightly at the burping hour, unremarkable, punctual, his.

Halfway is when the first year's shape becomes visible, the way a road's shape shows from a hill's middle. Behind: the fourth trimester's fog, the renovation, the launching, the feast — each impossible at the time, each folded now into the hands' plain knowledge. Ahead, the almanac forecast: the crawl in earnest, the pull to stand, the words, the steps — the year's loud half, arriving to a household that had, without ceremony, become good at this.

"Six months," said Papa to the porch.

"Maybe," said Mama, by which she meant — and he heard it entire, the dialect long since fluent between them — that time was doing the thing time does, both speeds at once, the days long and the months gone, and that she would take this exact porch, this exact weight on this exact knee, over any previous version of her whole life.

The streetlights came on down the lane, right on the season's new schedule.

Inside, later, the almanac took one line in two hands: Halfway. All well. All astonishing. Both true, still.

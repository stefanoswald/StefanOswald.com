Night 271

The Longest Evening

Month 9 — The Edge of Everything

The solstice arrived at the month's turning — the year's longest light, the sun still doing business at the liturgy's own appointed hour — and the household ran the annual negotiation the season files against every early bedtime in the northern towns.

The dispute, as the nursery heard it: seven o'clock, per the architecture's whole standing order — and the window's edges running stubborn gold behind the blackout's best efforts, the lane outside audibly still in session (the older children of Maple Lane at their evening franchise, the porch commerce running late), and the citizen at the crib rail entering the evidence with counsel's full energy: light. Others. Awake. The petition, denied nightly, refiled nightly.

The page runs the season's doctrine, second edition, for the households at their own gold-edged curtains. The blackout's investment, compounding again per the early-waking era's old arithmetic — the curtains' seal audited and reinforced (the almanac logs one strip of shameless tape at the leaking edge; the page endorses it without apology; June is June). The liturgy leaned on at full faith per the time-change's proven law: the sequence is the clock — bath, book, song running the body down its hallway of doors whatever the sun deposes, the sleep pressure's own arithmetic (the day's walking mileage now considerable) closing the case that the light kept opening. The bright evenings spent down, not fought: the day's last hour moved outdoors per the summer's own logic — the yard's wading tub, the lane's walk, the light burned as fuel rather than endured as argument — the solstice bedtime arriving through a citizen already spent at the season's own expense.

And the doctrine's soft clause, entered per the veterans' counsel and the household's own second-summer ease: the hour bends in June, and the bending is not collapse. The liturgy held its sequence at quarter-past some nights, half-past on the solstice's own; the architecture flexed per the chalk-not-ink constitution and trued itself as the light turned — because the light, the page notes per the whole year's deepest schedule, was already turning: the solstice being the one evening of the year that is only ever the far edge, the days shortening toward the candle's own season from the very next morning.

The petition ran twenty minutes at the rail on the year's longest evening, and settled per the sequence's old spell mid-song, the gold still at the curtain's edges, the lane still faintly in session, the sleeper down at last in the bright.

"Longest one," said Mama at the door, to the gold edges, to the year.

The light turned the next morning, per its ancient paperwork, three seconds shorter.

Toward the candle now. The almanac, keeping its own solstice, had already ruled the page:

Ten weeks minus one. The days count down in gold from here.

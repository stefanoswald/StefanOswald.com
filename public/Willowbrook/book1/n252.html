Night 252

The Vocabulary of One

Month 9: The Edge of Everything

The week after the pond, the household lived inside a linguistic era the texts call the holophrase, and the page documents it as the year's best comedy running on its deepest machinery.

The estate: one noun. (Two by the week's end; the era's arithmetic held regardless.) And the noun did every job in the language, because at this age one word is a sentence, the single coin minted at the pond now spent across the entire economy of intent, its meaning carried by aim, register, and the whole gesturing body per the wardrobe's year of infrastructure.

The almanac's field recordings, filed with translations. "Duck" (at the bathtub, imperative register, pointing): bring Esquire; the bath is incomplete. "Duck" (at the bear book, business register): the mallard spread; you know the one. "Duck!" (at the window, breaking-news register, both arms): a bird, any bird; the category's borders, per the era's charming science, running generous (the crow prosecuted as duck all week; the neighbor's cat, once, at dusk, the market issuing a gentle correction at each trade, "that's a crow, caw caw," the borders redrawing on the answers per the engine's standard method). "Duck?" (at the empty pond station on Thursday's walk, rising register, palms up): the era's masterpiece, a question, an absence noted, a whole clause of where are they? built from one coin and the body's full grammar.

The page files the era's linguistics under its comedy. The holophrase is language's true first act, the word not yet a brick in sentences but a whole building compressed, the child running meaning at sentence scale through a one-word aperture, and the listener's job (the parents certified in it within days) being the expansion: each coin received and returned built out, "Duck! Yes, the duck is swimming. Where did the ducks go? Maybe they're sleeping." The expansions, the language literature's oldest counsel, are the era's premium fuel: the child banks the built-out edition every time, the warehouse stocking sentence-shapes months before shipping them.

And the era's tenderness, entered at the week's close: the household had waited a year for words, and the first one's week revealed the truth every family learns at this window, the words were never going to be information; the household had run on fluent understanding for months. The words were company: the world, named aloud, together, in chorus, the whole year's beam, given a voice on both ends at last.

"Duck," offered the resident at lights-out, at the ceiling, in the settling register, the day's last coin, spent on nothing, or on everything.

"Duck," agreed the state, in kind.

It was, both parties understood, a complete conversation.

In that era, it always was.

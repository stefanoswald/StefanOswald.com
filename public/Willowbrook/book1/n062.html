Night 62

The Shape of a Day

Month 3: Rhythm

Somewhere in the third month, the days developed bones.

Not a schedule, nothing printable, nothing to defend at parties. But a shape: wake, feed, play, sleep, and around again, like a small dependable water wheel. The morning nap grew a usual hour. The bedtime landed in the same valley most nights. Even the fusses turned semi-official, arriving like a commuter train, tired but on time.

The books argue about this stage, loudly. Strict schedules! Total freedom! The third path, the one that fits most actual houses, is rhythm: same order of events, flexible clock. The order teaches the baby what comes next; the flexible clock forgives the day its weather.

For the parents, the bones changed everything. You could plan a walk now. Warn a visitor off the nap hour. Sit down at eight-fifteen most nights, look at another adult, and finish a sentence, a whole one, with a verb.

"We have a Tuesday," Papa said in wonder, looking at the calendar. "It resembles our Monday."

Wake, feed, play, sleep. Turn, turn, turns the wheel.

Olivia rode it like a queen on a slow carousel, and the days held her shape.

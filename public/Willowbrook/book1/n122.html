Night 122

Her Own Name

Month 5 — Nearly Sitting, Nearly Everything

"Olivia."

It was said across the kitchen, mid-task, not even aimed — and the small head came around like a compass finding north.

Mama went still. Said it again, softer: "Olivia." And again the turn, the seeking face, the unmistakable now-what of someone answering to something.

Somewhere in the fifth and sixth months, this door opens: the name, heard hundreds of times a day since birth, stops being weather and becomes address. Not understanding, yet — she knew the sound belonged to good things happening nearby, not that it was her, the way the mirror baby was not yet her. But the sound had been sorted from the stream, flagged, filed under respond. The first word she would ever truly own, and she was learning it the only way words are learned: by ten thousand deliveries, each one wrapped in a face.

The house, discovering the trick, immediately overused it, as houses do. Olivia from the doorway. Olivia at the changing table. Olivia sung in three registers by a father testing acoustics. Each turn a small astonishment, none of them wearing out.

The page's quiet counsel: keep the name in rotation with love attached — names land deepest when they mostly mean someone is glad you exist. Save the other tone for years from now, and use it seldom even then.

"Olivia," said Papa at bath time, experimentally, from behind her.

The whole wet head swiveled. Found him. Beamed.

Addressed, and answering. The postal system of the self: now open for delivery.

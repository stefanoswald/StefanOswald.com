Night 189

Harry in the Leaf Pile

Month 7 — Explorers and Guardrails

The backyard's one great oak had spent October letting go, and Saturday was raking day: Papa on the rake, Mama on cocoa logistics, and the republic's citizen stationed at the yard blanket's border, supervising the harvest with full-arm points and commentary.

The leaf pile grew to civic proportions. And from its base, at the hour when the light goes long and honey-colored, the pile — rustled.

Out trundled a hedgehog.

Caramel-spiked, purposeful, one yellow leaf riding his back like a knapsack he had given up negotiating with, he emerged on urgent hedgehog business — winter quarters being a serious portfolio in November — and found himself, four inches out, nose to nose with a baby.

Both parties froze. The lookback fired: Olivia's head swung to Mama — are you seeing this? — and swung back, arm rising to the full point, the beam thrown wide open on the astonishing fact of him. The hedgehog, for his part, performed the risk assessment of his cautious nation: considered rolling, read the blanket-anchored border and the unhurried giants, and concluded — this being Willowbrook, where the animals keep their own counsel about such things — that the small loud one was licensed weather, not threat.

He trundled on, unrolled, the whole yard's length, leaf aboard, while the beam tracked him to the fence gap and the point held like a lighthouse.

"Hedgehog," Mama named, inside the triangle. "Harry the hedgehog," she added, on instinct, the way names arrive in that town.

The page's small print, for yards less enchanted: wild visitors are watched, never handled — the naming beam is the whole safe transaction, distance being kindness in both directions, and the leaf pile checked henceforth before any burning or great collapse, hedgehogs and their kin wintering in exactly such civic works.

The pile was left standing that year, by unanimous vote, resident presumed.

The fence gap was watched all winter from the window, point at the ready.

He would be back. The town's records — kept in wainscoting, willow bark, and starlight — already had him down as hers.

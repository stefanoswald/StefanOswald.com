Night 77

The Bench of Parents

Month 3: Rhythm

There is a bench at every park, and on it sits the rest of the village, waiting to be met.

Mama took her stroller past it three walks in a row before the fourth walk, when the woman with the auburn curls scooted over and said the secret password, which is: "How's the sleep?"

Her name was Rosa. Her daughter, Lily, slept in the other stroller with her fists thrown over her head, born eleven days after Olivia, a fact the two mothers traded like children matching cards. What followed was forty-five minutes of the conversation new parents are starving for: the one where nothing has to be explained. The witching hour. The laundry mountain. The 3 a.m. breathing check, "you too?" "everyone," said Rosa, "everyone, forever."

This page is the nudge: sit on the bench. Say the password, or answer it. New parenthood is famously the loneliest crowded time there is, never alone, never accompanied, and the cure is not advice or apps. It is one other adult in the same boat, rowing beside you, laughing at the same waves.

Phone numbers were exchanged. A walking date was set, Tuesdays.

In their strollers, sixty combined days old, Olivia and Lily slept through their own introduction, the first meeting nobody would remember, before the one everybody would.

The bench had done its ancient work. It creaked, satisfied.

Night 153

The First Spoonful

Month 6 — The Feast Begins

Noon arrived. The chair was boarded, the bib deployed at heavy-weather rating, the camera propped and rolling, and history was mashed with a fork.

Iron-rich, the menu's first law, and the kitchen had obliged: a fortified cereal thinned with her own milk to the consistency of familiarity — first foods carrying the old flavor forward as escort. (The neighboring kingdoms start otherwise, and equally well: mashed meats at some tables, lentils at others, iron leading everywhere.)

The spoon approached, half-loaded, unhurried.

Contact.

The face that followed deserved its own page in the almanac and got one: the eyes widening, the lips working, the brow convening an emergency committee — outrage and intrigue at full parliamentary volume, simultaneously, over one gram of cereal. The mouth opened again.

Whether for protest or seconds, the record shows only: opened. The second spoonful entered under the same ambiguity. The third was met partway.

The page's counsel for every first feast: the plate is the least of it. First meals measure in grams and succeed in minutes — three spoonfuls is a banquet; one is a start; a refusal entire is Tuesday, retried Thursday without a mark on any record. Milk remains the kingdom's food supply for months yet — solids begin as ambassadors, not armies. And the face, that magnificent conflicted face, is not a verdict. It is news being processed. All new countries look like that from the border.

The banquet closed at four spoonfuls, the fifth conducted violently into the bib by a gesturing fist.

The camera had it all. The bib had most of it.

"Welcome to food," said Papa, wiping down the delegation.

The delegation pounded the tray: the gavel. More was tabled for tomorrow. The border stayed open for good.

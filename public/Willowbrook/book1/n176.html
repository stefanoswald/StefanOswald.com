Night 176

The Gallery of Food Faces

Month 6 — The Feast Begins

The feast's third month had assembled, at the rate of two exhibitions weekly, the household's finest art collection: the food faces.

The gallery's wings, curated by the almanac. The Betrayal Wing: green beans (the founding piece — the shudder running shoulder to shoulder, the lips withdrawing diplomatic relations), lemon's accidental masterpiece (one licked wedge, the whole face folding inward like a star collapsing, then — the astonishing coda — reaching for more). The Suspicion Wing: eggs, first sitting, examined mid-air with the gaze of a customs auditor; yogurt, approached across three meals like détente. The Rapture Wing, permanent collection: sweet potato in state, banana at court, the broth of soup weather, peach — peach had required a moment alone afterward, all parties.

The page hangs its counsel among the frames. The faces are data, not verdicts — the shudder is novelty processing, not policy, and the research's famous number rides here: a new food may need eight, ten, fifteen exhibitions before acceptance, each rejected showing still a showing, the palate's education running on exposure the way the babble ran on turns. The parental art is the curator's, not the salesman's: keep hanging the show — one bite's worth, no siege, no bargaining, the beloved anchor food alongside — and let the votes drift the way votes drifted for broccoli (rehabilitated, week nine, without explanation, as governments fall).

And the second counsel, softer: laugh later. The face meeting green beans reads the room; a table roaring at the shudder books the shudder for encores. Deadpan at the debut, hilarity at the recap — the curator's discipline, imperfectly kept in this house, the almanac admits, by one father in particular.

Green beans hung in the Betrayal Wing all autumn.

Acceptance came at Thanksgiving, ballot eleven, unremarked by the electorate and toasted silently by the board.

The gallery remains open. Admission: one bite. The collection, like its subject, still growing.

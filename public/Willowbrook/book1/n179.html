Night 179

Chasing the Light

Month 6 — The Feast Begins

The light thinned all month, the sun clocking off earlier each week, and the house learned the season's quiet arithmetic: less day, more indoors, and a certain flatness creeping into the afternoons that neither resident could name.

The remedy, this page reports, was the oldest one, adjusted for latitude: chase the light.

The walks moved to the light's own schedule — the former catnap hour, now the day's brightest remainder, claimed for the outing before the outing became impossible. Bundled per the driveway doctrine, breath visible, cheeks pinking on schedule, they took the town at its winter pace: shorter loops, slower, the stroller's passenger tracking her narrowed world with undimmed appetite — bare branches against grey sky (contrast cards, nature's edition), breath-clouds (pursued by mitten, uncatchable), the streetlights waking one by one down the lane, each ignition received as breaking news, nightly, without staleness.

The arithmetic beneath: daylight, even grey daylight, is dosage — the small clock winds on it, the night sleep organizing around days that contain actual day, and the parent's own weather (the book has said it since month two, and winter says it louder) lifting measurably per outdoor hour. The vitamin the sun ships is on the doctor's docket besides, the drops prescribed at this latitude's winters doing the chemical half while the walk does the rest.

And the blue hour itself — that long dusk the season serves early — turned out to hold the winter's secret gift: the town at lamplight, the windows going gold one by one, Willowbrook wearing its evening face for an audience of one stroller, nightly, free.

Home through full dark, the last lamp theirs.

The flatness, chased daily, kept losing.

Winter would press its case for months. The stroller had its answer, and kept the appointment: same hour, same loop, chasing the light clear through to spring.

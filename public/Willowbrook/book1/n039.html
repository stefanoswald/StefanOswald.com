Night 39

The Hour That Has No Reason

Month 2 — Smiles and Sleep

Some evenings had a reason — hungry, gassy, overtired. And some evenings there was simply crying, roaring along like a train with no stations, and every trick bounced off.

The doctor had a rule of threes for the biggest version: crying three hours, three evenings a week, three weeks running, in a baby otherwise thriving — that is colic, and here is the part parents need stitched into their sleeve: it is nobody's fault. Not the milk, not the parenting, not some message being missed. Colicky babies grow into perfectly happy children who owe everyone an apology they will never remember to give.

Peak crying comes around six weeks in most babies, colic or not, then genuinely fades by three or four months. It is a season, with an end printed on the ticket.

Survival was simple and unglamorous. Trade shifts before the fraying starts. Sway, shush, step outside — evening air confuses crying, sometimes wonderfully. And when both pairs of arms are spent, the safe crib and five minutes of hallway breathing, always allowed.

That night the train ran an hour, then simply stopped, the way it always eventually did.

Silence. Blessed, ringing silence. They stood in it like rain after a drought.

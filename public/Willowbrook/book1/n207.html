Night 207

The Deep Winter Rhythm

Month 7 — Explorers and Guardrails

January settled onto Willowbrook like a quilt with opinions, and the household entered the deep winter rhythm — the page's name for the season's particular art, filed here as its own small chapter of parenting craft.

The rhythm's terms: dark by late afternoon, outings rationed by weather, the same rooms serving all functions, week upon week. The hazards, honestly named: the walls' slow shrink (the parent's old enemy from the second month, returned in its winter uniform), the sameness that flattens afternoons, the cabin fever that arrives disguised as everyone being vaguely terrible by four p.m.

The craft, assembled by trial across the month. Anchors, two per day, immovable: the morning's chased light (the walk taken in nearly all weather per the northern doctrine, brief and bundled, the daylight dose banked before noon) and the afternoon's named event — fortress day, bath-brought-forward day, cupboard reorganization summit, the video-call grandfather on his standing Tuesdays. Between anchors, the floor curriculum ran itself as ever: the putting-in industries, the book in shuffle mode, the pot orchestra's winter season.

And the fourth-p.m. hour — the flattest hour, the whole town's worst — met with the craft's best trick: change the room. Any change. Lights on early and warm. The music put on (the dancing era having arrived on schedule: the bounce-in-place at any rhythm, the house obliged to provide rhythms). The bath run at four-thirty on the terrible days, hang the schedule — warm water resetting small weather like nothing else in the winter kit.

The parents ran their own winter maintenance per the season's quiet requirements: the walk taken even solo on traded mornings, the bench friendships kept indoors at kitchen tables, the sun lamp Rosa swore by installed at the breakfast counter, the flatness watched — per the month-two page, still binding — for the two-week line where weather becomes weather that should be said to someone.

The rhythm held the month. The month, held, passed.

Under the fence line, the leaf-roofed sleeper held his own winter rhythm, breathing four times a minute, waiting for the same spring.

It was coming. The light, chased daily, was already three minutes longer. The almanac had started counting.

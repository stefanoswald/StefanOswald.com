Night 66

Grip

Month 3 — Rhythm

The rattle was offered. The rattle was seized.

Not the old reflex-grab of the newborn weeks — that automatic curl, sweet and mindless as a fern. This was on purpose: fingers opening in advance, aiming, closing around the wooden ring with the satisfaction of a door latching. Hers now. All things were hers now, potentially, and all things had one destiny: the mouth.

The mouth is not a bad habit; at this age it is the laboratory. Lips and tongue carry finer sensors than fingertips for now, so everything graspable goes in for analysis — texture, temperature, chewability, general character. The parental job is not to stop the science but to curate the specimens: big enough to never swallow (the toilet-paper-tube test — through the tube means too small), clean enough, nothing with button batteries or loose parts, ever.

Good specimens: wooden rings, silicone leaves, cloth blocks, the eternal burp cloth. All approved, all analyzed, all found delicious.

Olivia gummed the rattle with the focus of a jeweler.

"Findings?" Papa asked.

She shook it. It rattled. She startled magnificently, recovered, and shook it again on purpose.

Cause, meet effect. The laboratory would work late tonight.

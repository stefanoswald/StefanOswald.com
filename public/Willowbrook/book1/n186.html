Night 186

The Stairs Summit Conference

Month 7 — Explorers and Guardrails

The stairs had been gated since the great expedition — top and bottom, screwed to studs, the audit's proudest infrastructure — and for two months the gates had merely been furniture.

This month they became the border of an obsession.

She discovered the staircase the way explorers discover forbidden ranges: through the bars, gazing up, both fists on the gate, issuing the new gesture — the whole-arm point — at the summit, with commentary. Daily. The stairs were the finest climbing object in the house and the only one embargoed, a mathematics every crawler works out instantly and files under injustice.

The house's answer, this page's counsel for every gated household: don't only forbid the mountain — teach it. The gates hold the border ninety-nine hours of the week; the hundredth hour, supervised, the border opens for lessons. Papa's academy convened Sunday mornings: the climb up, taught first (crawlers ship with upward instincts, hands-and-knees on the treads, a parent's hand hovering at the hull), summited in a fortnight to gavel-pounding self-acclaim. Then the descent — the counterintuitive curriculum, backward, feet first, taught by turning her at the top and guiding the reverse gear until it compiled — because the descent is the skill that saves, the one a child needs installed for the inevitable day a gate stands open, and backward-down is the only gear that lands.

(The page stamps the fine print: lessons are supplements, not substitutes. The gates stay commissioned, latched, and screwed until deep into the walking years; taught climbers are safer climbers, not licensed ones.)

By month's end she climbed the full flight in lesson conditions like a small ranked alpinist, and descended backward with the smugness of dual certification.

The gates held all other hours, rattled daily, cursed in babble.

The mountain, visible through bars, remained the house's best-loved injustice — and its best-taught one, which was the entire treaty.

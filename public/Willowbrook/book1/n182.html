Night 182

The Speed Upgrade

Month 7 — Explorers and Guardrails

The crawl shipped its second version this month, and the household's entire security model required review.

Version one had been deliberate: the four-beat gait, destinations achieved on geologic schedules, a parent able to finish a sentence mid-transit. Version two was velocity. Somewhere in the seventh month the gait compiled — the beats blurring into flow, the knees learning their trade, the hands slapping tile in a drumroll — and she crossed the kitchen in the time it took to turn off a faucet. The hallway became a runway. The distance between glanced-at and gone compressed to four seconds, measured twice by a father with a stopwatch and a new respect.

The page files the era's revised security doctrine. The audit rerun monthly, as promised, now at speed-adjusted assumptions: not what can she reach but what can she reach in the time a kettle takes. The bathroom door, formerly optional, now closed by standing order — toilets and tubs being the wet hazards the safety boards rank highest at exactly this mobility. The stair gates, tested by lean and rattle, holding their commissions. And the supervision model upgraded from line-of-sight to zone defense: rooms pre-cleared, gates deployed, the yes spaces earning their charters hourly — because eyes alone lose to version two, and the environment must hold what attention cannot.

The zone defense's dividend, noted in fairness: within held borders, freedom at full throttle. She ranged, drummed, raided her cupboard, commuted between provinces — liberty and security in the same architecture, which had been the constitution's whole idea since the first gate went up.

Top recorded speed that month: the length of the hall in the time of one sneeze.

The stopwatch retired. The doors, henceforth, counted themselves.

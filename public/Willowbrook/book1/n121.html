Night 121

The Tripod Holds

Month 5 — Nearly Sitting, Nearly Everything

The pillow court had trained her; the fifth month graduated her.

One ordinary morning, set down on the rug for the usual wobbling session, Olivia planted both hands, stacked her spine above them, lifted her head into the sunlight — and stayed. No slow lean. No sideways settle into the cushions. The tripod, holding, minute after minute, while she looked around at her realm from the fixed and unprecedented altitude of herself.

Supported sitting was becoming plain sitting, the way it does in the fifth and sixth months: first the tripod, hands on duty, then — glimpsed already in flashes — the hands-free version, torso balancing alone while the hands went off to handle toys, which is the entire point of the tower. Sitting is not really about sitting. It is about freeing the staff.

The parental arts stayed humble. Pillows still ringing the realm, since new towers fall backward without notice. Sessions on her schedule, ended at the wobble's first slur. And the floor kept honest beneath her — practice on carpet and rug, not sofa cushions, firm ground being the only true teacher of balance.

She sat through an entire toy rotation that morning, working the ring at the new altitude, gravity's oldest student now somehow on the faculty.

Then one grand backward flop into the pillows, received like an opinion.

The tower would rebuild tomorrow. Towers do, at this age, daily, until one day they simply stand.

Night 170

The Two-Nap Treaty

Month 6 — The Feast Begins

The catnap died quietly, the way small institutions do: skipped once, protested nothing, skipped twice, unmissed.

For a fortnight the signs had gathered. The late-afternoon third nap — the little bridge that had carried her to bedtime since spring — kept shrinking, then kept being fought, then began costing more than it paid: taken, it shoved bedtime late into gravel; refused, the evening somehow held anyway. The two remaining naps, meanwhile, stretched and steadied like heirs coming into property.

The two-nap treaty was signed on a Tuesday, terms as follows. A morning nap, midmorning now rather than early, an hour or so of good faith. An afternoon anchor, early-afternoon, the day's long keel. Wake windows lengthened to match — two and a half hours, sometimes three, the ninety-minute era formally antique. And bedtime pulled a half hour earlier through the transition, the treaty's standard bridging clause, since droppers of naps run a sleep deficit until the new schedule vests.

The page timestamps the change for consulting households: the three-to-two transition commonly lands in the six-to-nine-month country, negotiated exactly this way — the third nap fading, fighting, costing — and the transition weeks run ragged by design. Some days need the old third nap; some days refuse it; the treaty permits both without renegotiation. Follow the baby; trim the edges; hold the anchor holy.

By the second week the new architecture stood square: two rooms, well-built, the day's shape simpler and somehow larger.

The chalkboard was redrawn, two blocks where three had been, in chalk as ever. Nothing in ink. The almanac had taught them that much: schedules are weather reports, not constitutions.

The former catnap hour became, of all things, the second walk.

Autumn approved. So, sleeping ninety minutes at the anchor now, did she.

Night 168

The Autumn Coat Question

Month 6: The Feast Begins

The first true cold snap raised the season's most misunderstood safety question, and this page settles it in the driveway, buckle by buckle.

The puffy coat, gift of a doting aunt, adorable past argument, went on for the walk to the car. At the car, it came off. The harness cinched against thin fleece layers, pinch-tested at the collarbone, snug on the actual child. And then the coat, reversed, draped over the buckled straps like a wearable blanket, arms available, warmth restored.

The physics earn one plain paragraph: puffy insulation is mostly air, and air compresses. Straps pinch-tight over a lofted coat are, in the arithmetic of a sudden stop, inches loose, the coat flattening on impact, the child moving inside a harness fitted to a garment that is no longer there. The crash-test footage that taught the rule is unavailable to nurseries and should stay so; the rule itself travels fine without it: buckle the baby, blanket the coat.

The cold-season kit assembled around the rule as the week went on. The fleece layers that compress honestly. The hat that stayed on (the second hat war, autumn theater, settled by the same treaty as the first: solidarity and distraction). The car warmed ahead when the driveway allowed. And the old chest-check ported forward from the newborn pages, seasonally adjusted: a hand down the collar at destination, warm and dry, correct; damp at the neck, one layer lighter next trip, the bundled baby overheating faster than the walking parent ever suspects.

Olivia rode to the farm that Sunday snug at the collarbone, coat riding shotgun as blanket, cheeks cold-pinked and grinning at the gold going past the window.

Autumn had questions. The driveway had answers. The pinch test, as ever, held the casting vote.

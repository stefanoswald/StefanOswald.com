Night 247

The Audience Effect

Month 9: The Edge of Everything

The walking's practice hours revealed, mid-month, a law the household came to call the audience effect, and the page passes it along because every skill of the raising years obeys it.

The evidence, gathered from doorways. Watched, the arms-open game, the demonstrations, the come-to-me theater, the walker performed at performance's rates: three steps and the catch, the wobble amplified by the very attention, the falls arriving faster under the audience's weight. Unwatched, glimpsed from the hallway's unnoticed angle, tracked in the low mirror's periphery, she trained: laps of the yes space at seven and nine strides, the squat-and-rise drilled in sets, the turn (the gait's advanced course) attempted, failed, attempted, landed, a private athletics conducted daily, for no one, at levels the public shows never touched.

And the moment of discovery, replicated enough times for the law's certification: the watchers noticed, and the training stopped, the sit-down prompt, the face changing registers, the private athlete converting on the spot to the public performer or (the era's other option, equally honored) the show refused entire, the walker freighting off crawlwise to less-observed markets.

The page holds the law up to the light, because its print runs finer than comedy. Skills consolidate in private, the learning literature confirms at every age, the audience loading the machinery with exactly the self-attention new skills cannot yet carry, and the child's own practice-scheduling, the solo drills at her own chosen edge, is the mechanism the whole frustration-fuel doctrine was always protecting. The parental arts of the audience era, accordingly: watch more from doorways, applaud more at completions than during attempts (the mid-stride cheer, the almanac confirmed, dropped the walker at rates approaching total), and hold the performances lightly, the child who won't show the grandmother the walking is not behind; she is guarding her laboratory, per sound practice everywhere.

And the law's tender corollary, entered at the week's end: the doorway watching became its own institution, the quiet-ten-minutes doctrine's mobile edition, and banked what the demonstrations never could: the athlete at her actual work, unperformed, entire. The year's truest footage, the almanac notes, was all shot from hallways.

The evening's private session ran eleven strides, a turn landed on the third attempt, and one squat-rise-carry cycle with the bear as cargo, a triple no audience had ever been offered.

The doorway held its breath and its applause, per the law.

The athlete trained on, sovereign, unwatched, magnificent.

The page pulls the door gently to, and follows her example.

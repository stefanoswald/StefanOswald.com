Night 111

The Dropping Game

Month 4: Rolling and Laughing

The spoon went over the side of the high chair, and Papa picked it up.

The spoon went over the side, and Papa picked it up.

The spoon, you see the shape of the evening. By the ninth retrieval, Papa understood he had been drafted into the fourth month's great research program: the Dropping Game, played in every high chair on earth, staffed always by one small scientist and one large unpaid assistant.

It looks like mischief. It is physics, at the doctoral level, for the enrolled age. Release, the deliberate opening of the hand, itself a skill newer than the grip. Fall, always down, never up, confirmed across all known spoons. Sound, the clatter, varying delightfully by object, a percussion section commanded from a chair. And the crowning discovery, tested most of all: retrieval. The thing goes, and the assistant restores it. Gone, then back. The peekaboo truth again, in cutlery form: what leaves can return.

The assistant's contract, this page advises, permits limits. Three retrievals cheerfully, then the tether toy takes the shift, or the program adjourns to floor-based studies. Science respects office hours.

Drop ten concluded the evening's trials. The scientist peered over the edge at the spoon, then at Papa, with a look of complete professional confidence.

He picked it up. Of course he picked it up.

The results were reproducible. That is the whole tragedy and glory of assistants.

Night 177

The First Fever

Month 6 — The Feast Begins

It announced itself at the 2 a.m. feed: the heat coming off her like a small stove, the cheeks flushed past sleep's usual pink, the whimper with a new note in it.

The thermometer confirmed what the collarbone already knew. Fever — the first true one, the cold of month three having run cool throughout. And the house, drilled for exactly this by the refrigerator list and the six-month conference, ran the night on doctrine instead of dread.

The doctrine, printed here in full for every 2 a.m. that finds this page. The number matters less than the child past the newborn line: a miserable baby at modest fever outranks a playful one at higher — watch the baby, not the mercury. The true tripwires, memorized: the fever in any baby under three months (call, always, at once); the limpness, the unconsolable hours, the labored breathing, the dry diapers mounting, the rash that doesn't blanch, the instinct that says wrong — each one a call, day or night, no apologies, pediatricians keeping night phones for precisely this. Short of the tripwires: fluids pressed gently, the milk on demand all night; dress light, no bundling a stove; the fever-reducer at the doctor-blessed dose if misery asks it (comfort's medicine, not cure's — the fever itself is the immune system working, not the enemy); and the vigil, in the chair, in shifts.

The vigil ran till five, the small stove against Mama's chest, the doctrine holding the room steadier than she felt.

By morning: ninety-nine and falling, appetite filing grievances, the pediatrician's nurse confirming the course run true — a virus of the season, passing through, the first of childhood's licensed hundred.

By dinner the patient governed her cupboard at full volume, restored, amnesiac.

The parents aged the customary amount and recovered on the customary schedule: slower, gratefully, over soup.

The list went back on the refrigerator. The doctrine, tested, held. It would be needed again; it would hold again. That is what doctrines are for.

Night 155

The Allergen Calendar

Month 6 — The Feast Begins

The old advice had been a fortress: delay the perilous foods — the peanut, the egg — for years, behind walls.

The new science, and this page carries it plainly, tore the fortress down: early and often won. The immune system, met with the great allergens in the first year — around six months, once first foods run smooth — learns them as food; met late, it too often learns them as threat. The peanut studies rewrote the books outright: early introduction, dramatic reductions. The doctor had said as much at the six-month visit, with the standing asterisk — babies with severe eczema or an egg allergy already on file take the same journey with a pediatric guide, sometimes tested first, always coached.

So the calendar went up, and the campaign ran orderly. One new player at a time, three or so days apart, mornings for preference — reactions, if any, given daylight to declare themselves. Peanut, first: a smear of the smooth stuff thinned to paste in cereal (whole nuts and thick gobs staying on the forbidden list; the allergen enters, the choke hazard does not). Then egg, well-cooked, mashed. Then, in their weeks: wheat, dairy in its yogurt costume, soy, fish, the sesame the new lists promoted.

The watch, each debut: the skin (hives, spreading redness), the gut (vomiting past the ordinary), the breathing (the one that calls emergency, not the pediatrician). And the note the calendar kept faithfully: introduced is not finished — the allergens, once landed, stay in the rotation, the immune system's education being a standing enrollment, not a single lecture.

Peanut day passed without event: tasted, worn slightly, approved absolutely.

The calendar took its checkmark. The fortress era closed, one thinned smear at a time.

The eater, unconsulted on the science, filed peanut under more.

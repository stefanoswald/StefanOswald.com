Night 134

Dinner Theater

Month 5 — Nearly Sitting, Nearly Everything

Dinner had become theater, and the fifth month promoted its audience to the cast.

She had a spoon of her own now — pre-loaded with nothing, a prop, granted after the Dropping Game's contract negotiations — and she conducted with it. Conversation swung across the table; the spoon followed, waving at each speaker in turn, a maestro cueing her orchestra. When laughter broke out, she joined at volume, cause unknown, timing impeccable. When a lull fell, she filled it: "Ba-ba-ba. Ma-ma-ba," delivered with the gravity of a toast.

The babble had gone social, that was the news — no longer practiced on lamps alone but aimed, into the turn-taking, at the table's rhythm. She had the music of conversation before a single word of its lyrics: the pause that means your turn, the rising note that asks, the register that jokes. Researchers call it protoconversation, and its best rehearsal hall is exactly this — the family meal, nightly, where the rhythms play in surround.

The table's arts, for parents keeping such theater: leave her the pauses, answer the toasts ("Ba? A bold position. Papa disagrees"), and let her hold the floor when she takes it, the same courtesies that build talkers being the ones that build diners.

Watching food remained the subplot — tonight's potatoes tracked like a courtroom exhibit, the reach now practiced daily. The feast lay one month out, near enough to smell.

The maestro's spoon hit the tray at the meal's end: the gavel, calling curtain.

Cast party at bath time. Everyone was invited. Everyone came.

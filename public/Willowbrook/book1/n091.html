Night 91

The Comedy Season Opens

Month 4 — Rolling and Laughing

The fourth month opened the comedy season, and the whole house bought season tickets.

What had been one laugh, mined with effort, was now a repertoire. The cushion pop — Papa vanishing behind the couch arm and surging up with a "boo" — reliable as sunrise. The zerbert on the belly: a standing ovation every time. The dropped-jaw gasp of fake surprise; the slow tickle spider walking up the ribs announcing itself the whole way, the announcement being most of the joke.

Babies this age laugh at exactly one genre: expectation, gently broken. The face that should be there, gone — then back! The voice that should be normal, suddenly enormous. Comedy is surprise wearing a seatbelt, and the seatbelt is trust: she laughs because she knows, in her bones, that the surprise is safe.

Which is why the laughing matters more than it looks like it does. Every giggle is the nervous system practicing a magnificent sentence: the unexpected can be wonderful. There are adults still working on that sentence. She was drilling it nightly, in pajamas.

Tonight's set ran four encores of the cushion pop.

"We'll be here all year," Papa told his audience.

The audience fell asleep mid-show, the highest compliment in the business.

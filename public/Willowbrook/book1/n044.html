Night 44

The Narrator

Month 2 — Smiles and Sleep

"Now we are opening the refrigerator. Cold! And here is an apple — a red apple. Round, red apple. Now we are putting it back, because we actually needed butter."

Mama had become the narrator of the world's least eventful documentary, and its only viewer was riveted.

Talking to a baby feels absurd at first — a monologue delivered to someone who answers in vowels. But this is the actual mechanism by which language arrives: thousands of ordinary sentences, poured over ordinary moments, until the words and the world grow together. The baby who hears the running commentary — sock, water, up we go, warm — banks every syllable months before spending one.

The style matters less than the volume knob of love in it. That sing-song voice parents fall into, higher and slower and stretchy on the vowels? It has a scientific name, and babies prefer it the way ears prefer music.

"And now, the thrilling conclusion: butter, located."

"Ooh," said Olivia, with perfect timing.

Everyone is a critic. This one approved. The documentary was renewed for eighteen more years.

Night 196

The Nap Strike

Month 7 — Explorers and Guardrails

The two-nap treaty, ratified in the sixth month, faced its first constitutional crisis in the seventh: the strike.

The morning nap, approached by the full liturgy, was met at the crib by a citizen who did not lie down. Who stood — the new power, deployed now as politics — gripping the rail at full extension, protesting in chains and growls, a one-baby picket line at the treaty's own border. Twenty minutes of it. Then, some days, capitulation into sleep mid-protest, folded over the rail's shadow like a parliamentarian asleep at the podium. Other days: no nap at all, the session adjourned unslept, the afternoon paying the deficit in gravel.

The page identifies the suspects in the era's standing lineup. The skill storm, prime as ever: pulling-to-stand rehearses at naptime with special fury — the crib being the house's best rigging — the body drilling its new power exactly when asked to holster it. The treaty's own settling: two-nap architecture takes weeks to true up, the morning room still finding its walls. And the deeper current the charts flag at this age: practicing being a self that wants things, including not-this.

The doctrine held, adjusted. The liturgy unchanged — the road stays the road, the strike being weather upon it. The standing citizen laid back down once, twice, with boring calm, then left to negotiate with the mattress on her own authority (new powers, the page notes, often need to be exercised to exhaustion before they'll holster; the standing baby who cannot yet sit back down — a real mechanical gap some weeks — assisted matter-of-factly, then left to the republic's own business). The windows audited again, the morning nap nudged later as the treaty's fine print allowed. And the deficit days absorbed without forecast-catastrophizing, per the ancient 4 a.m. arithmetic rules, still binding.

The strike ran eleven days, tapering.

The treaty held, amended: morning nap at nine-forty now, by mutual exhaustion.

The almanac filed it under the growing list with a title the family would reuse for years: New powers are loud. Then they're just powers.

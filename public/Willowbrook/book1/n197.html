Night 197

Soup for the Strikers

Month 7 — Explorers and Guardrails

The strike's eleventh day was the one the page keeps, because it was the worst one, and the worst ones are where the book's real subject lives.

No morning nap. Anchor nap: forty minutes, ended by a garbage truck. The afternoon run on deficit gravel — the citizen fussing at everything and its opposite, held constantly, put down never, the cupboard government in shambles, the fortress declined, the day's plans reduced by noon to weather survival. Mama had been the whole state since six a.m., and when the door opened at dusk she was standing at the window doing the bounce with a baby who had just refused, in order, milk, the ring, and comfort itself.

What happened next took nine seconds and had taken seven months to build. Papa read the room from the doorframe — the particular stillness, the bounce's frayed rhythm — crossed it, and executed the relief in one practiced motion: the baby lifted from her arms mid-bounce, the handoff kiss landing on a tired cheek, and the words that were the whole technology: "I've got her. Soup's on in ten. Go be nowhere."

The page has written the shifts, the numbers said aloud, the treaty of the team of two, since the first month. This is what the clauses are for — not the schedulable hardness but the ambush kind, the day that simply exceeds its staffing. The skills of the relief, itemized for every second-shift reader: read the frayed bounce (asking how was the day mid-bounce is data collection; the lift is aid). Take the body first, the story after. Attach no evaluation to the wreckage — the strike was the striker's doing, and the state that held through it holds no case to answer. And soup — the literal kind when possible, but soup here stands for the whole category: the made thing, handed over, requiring nothing of the receiver.

Mama was nowhere for forty minutes: a bath with the door shut, the winter dark at the little window, the house's noise arriving pleasantly muffled, somebody else's.

She came down to soup, a fed baby, and the fortress rebuilt with the citizen inside it, gnawing the flap, restored to charm by the mere rotation of the guard.

"Day from the archives," she said.

"Filed," said Papa.

The almanac took one line, in his hand: Strike day 11. Held the line. Soup.

No lesson tonight, the page concludes, beyond the one the whole book rests on: the baby is raised by whatever the two of them keep alive between them. Feed that, and it feeds everything.

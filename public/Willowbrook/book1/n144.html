Night 144

The Long Distraction

Month 5 — Nearly Sitting, Nearly Everything

Feeding had developed a new difficulty, and the difficulty was the entire world.

Mid-feed, the world kept happening: a door, a dog two streets off, Papa's chair creaking in the next room — and Olivia would detach like a fire chief hearing the bell, swiveling toward the event, dinner abandoned mid-course. Thirty seconds of surveillance, return, three swallows, another bell. Feeds that had run twenty tranquil minutes now straggled across forty distracted ones.

The distractible feeder is the fifth month on schedule, this page reports, and she is not a problem — she is a nervous system succeeding. The same wiring that tracked the bear across the room now finds the whole house broadcast-worthy, and the feed, formerly the day's most interesting event, has competition it cannot always beat.

The remedies are environmental, not disciplinary. The boring room: feeds moved to the dim bedroom, door shut, world muffled — the fascinating house traded for beige walls, and appetite winning by default. The scheduled quiet: the biggest feeds banked at waking and bedtime, when the surveillance department is off shift. The nursing necklace or a cloth to grip, occupying the free hand that otherwise conducts the world's business. And the parental patience recalibrated: shorter, more frequent feeds are the era's shape, not a crisis of supply or interest.

The counsel underneath: this, too, is a season. The efficiency returns — babies become brutally quick, businesslike feeders by the year's turn, and the forty-minute wanderings get remembered, absurdly, fondly.

Tonight's dinner ran in the dim room, uninterrupted, both parties present for all courses.

The world, unwatched for twenty minutes, managed itself. It generally does.

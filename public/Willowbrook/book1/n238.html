Night 238

Ten Seconds, Standing

Month 8: The Velcro Weeks and the Thaw

The stand, unassisted, three seconds at its winter debut, five by the wagon's launch, crossed in the eighth month's last week the line the household had privately set as the era's summit marker: ten seconds, freestanding, mid-floor, no furniture in the arithmetic.

The page records the summit attempt in full, the almanac's account being the year's most closely written entry to date.

The setting: the living room interior, morning, the wagon docked at the far wall and its pilot disembarked mid-floor, the dismount itself now routine, the squat-to-sitting executed daily with a stevedore's economy. The ascent: from squatting, this time, without rigging, the deep-knee press to standing that the couch rail had taught and the floor now hosted solo, arms deployed at half-mast per the tightrope instrumentation. And the stand: held. Held through the wobble's first argument. Held through the second, the arms making their small corrections, the whole apparatus, vestibular department, ankle country, the core's long curriculum since the pillow court, running live and holding, while behind two coffee cups two adults counted in perfect silence, per the standing rule against sudden weather during miracles: seven, eight, nine...

Ten. Eleven. At thirteen seconds she noticed her audience, per the winter precedent; the notice cost the balance, per the same; the drop to the bottom executed to specification; and the applause, embargoed thirteen seconds, detonated at full house.

The stander joined it, seated, clapping for herself with the era's whole self-referential delight.

The page marks what the ten-second line means, for households counting at their own coffee: standing free at this duration is the last door before the door. The balance now holds without hands; the rise now happens without rigging; between the standing child and the walking one remains only the weight's first brave transfer, one foot unloaded, swung, landed, the step, the actual unthinkable, for which every syllabus in the book's second half has been the prerequisite. Some children take the door within days of the ten-second line; some stand at it for weeks, cruising and wagoning in full contentment, per their own almanacs as ever. The forecast pages of this one had said the spring side all along, and the spring was here, and the door stood open.

The morning's summit was celebrated per family custom at the level of moon landings: pancakes, the video-call grandfather informed out of Tuesday-turn ("Well now" delivered at its fullest documented register), the almanac's entry boxed.

Thirteen seconds. Mid-floor. She rose by herself.

The steps are in the building.

Below it, in the other handwriting, one line, the old dialect's entire vocabulary:

Maybe. ❤ Any day now. Maybe.

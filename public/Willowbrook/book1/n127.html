Night 127

Sunday at the Fence Line

Month 5 — Nearly Sitting, Nearly Everything

Sundays had settled into scripture: the drive out, the too-much lunch, and then Grandpa Joe's constitutional along the fence line, conducted this season with a deputy on his arm.

The chickens received them at the wire, and the introductions ran formal, as they had for weeks now. "That's Henrietta. Don't lend her money." Olivia stared at Henrietta with the entire front of her head. "The speckled one's Duchess. The Duchess situation is complicated." Each name delivered gravely, each bird considered at length by the deputy, whose research notes consisted of pumping arms and one long vowel of appreciation.

A farm, to a five-month-old, is the best-funded laboratory on earth. Animals at every altitude. Smells with no urban equivalent. The tractor, patted ritually, warm from the sun like a large red animal itself. And running under all of it, the old man's voice, narrating the world in his unhurried register — different from Mama's music and Papa's cello, a third instrument, weathered, and hers.

The page here is for the grandparents' pace itself. Research measures what farm Sundays deliver — language poured out unhurried, novelty at the dose that feeds rather than floods — but the realer finding needs no study: children stitched early into a slower generation inherit a second speed, and keep it somewhere, for life.

At the fence corner, the deputy's head grew heavy on the flannel shoulder.

"Meeting adjourned," Grandpa Joe told the chickens, who disagreed at volume.

He walked back the long way, deliberately. Some inheritances transfer best at two miles an hour.

Night 109

Milk Is Still Dinner

Month 4 — Rolling and Laughing

With the high chair seated and the audience installed, a reasonable question knocked at the fourth month's door: shouldn't she be eating something?

The aunts thought so, loudly. A little cereal in the bottle, they advised, for the sleeping. A taste of banana, for the fun. In their day, babies ate at three months and looked how everyone turned out.

This page holds the door, kindly and firmly. Milk — breast, bottle, either, both — is not the appetizer of the first six months. It is the entire menu, and it is enough: every calorie, every drop of drink, engineered to specification and updated continuously in ways no pantry can match. The early spoon steals more than it gives — displacing milk with lesser fare, asking a gut and a swallow that are not ready, and buying, the research shows plainly, not one extra minute of night sleep. The cereal-in-the-bottle trick is retired for good reasons, choking among them.

The sixth-month feast would come, with its readiness signs — sitting steady, the lost tongue-thrust, that unconcealed intent about the potatoes — and it would be glorious.

Until then: watching was the curriculum, milk was dinner, and dinner was, nightly, perfect.

Olivia finished hers at dusk, drunk as a lord on the complete menu, and slept like someone lacking nothing.

Because she lacked nothing. The menu said so, in every language milk is written in.

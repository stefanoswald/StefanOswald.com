Night 89

The Quarter Counted

Month 3 — Rhythm

One quarter of one year. They counted it on the couch with the lamp low, the way they counted months one and two, a small family tradition now three entries old.

The countables, first. One laugh, arrived and repeating. Rolls in both directions, the mat conquered entirely. A reach that meant it, hands promoted to staff, the swaddle honorably retired, a rhythm where the fog had been — naps with names, evenings with a shape, one bench friendship struck at the park with Tuesdays attached.

Then the uncountables, which took longer. The way the crying no longer frightened them, mostly, because it was language now and they were conversational. The way the house had stopped pretending it would be its old self and become, instead, this — the mat, the drying rack forest, the good chair angled at the window. The way each of them had watched the other, some hard 4 a.m., be kinder than the hour required, and had filed it away as the thing they now knew for certain about the person they had married.

"A quarter of a year," said Papa.

"Maybe," said Mama, which in her language meant: and all the rest of them, too.

Between them, the whole subject slept on, twelve pounds of accumulating future, one fist curled around Mama's sleeve like a signature on the quarter's accounts.

Audited. Approved. Carried forward, entire.

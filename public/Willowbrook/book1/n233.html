Night 233

The Balloon Over the Willows

Month 8: The Velcro Weeks and the Thaw

The Sunday farm liturgy, restored to full in-person service with the roads' spring clearing, was proceeding through its fence-line movement when the deputy stopped the procession cold: the arm up, the point at full lock, aimed past the chickens, past the pasture, at the willow line where the low sun was doing its gold work...

and above the willows, bobbing on the evening air like a note pinned to the sky: a red balloon.

"Well now," said Grandpa Joe, following the point, and his voice had the register the family had learned to read as the farm's own news service. "She's back, then."

The balloon traversed the willow line at walking pace, at, the record notes, precisely the walking pace of something large and gentle and unhurried beneath it, paused opposite the fence as if in acknowledgment, dipped once (the point tracking every degree of it, the lookback firing at the dip: confirm, confirm), and passed on behind the green, going home.

Ellie. Wintered wherever the balloon-tailed winter, the far barn country past the pasture, the town's records suppose, though the records, kept in wainscoting and willow bark, are famously discreet on the matter, and returned with the spring per the arrangement nobody at the farm had ever found remarkable.

The page, as at the first sighting, keeps the science it can and holds the rest lightly. That the eighth-month memory now spans a winter, the point aimed with recognition's full voltage, the texts confirm, at a file eight months old and never deleted, is developmental fact, and a quietly staggering one: the world's returning things returned now to someone keeping accounts. That new sights and old friends alike are the growing mind's best feasts, the book has held since the first Sunday. And that a town might keep an elephant behind its willows, unremarked, for the particular benefit of its youngest citizens...

that is Willowbrook's own business, and the book is only its guest.

"Ba!" said the deputy to the willow line, at volume, with the aimed conviction of her certified vocabulary.

The willows moved in the evening wind, gold and green, in the manner of willows, which is also, the page observes, very much the manner of a wave.

"Sundays," Grandpa Joe advised the willow line, resuming the procession. "We're here Sundays."

The balloon, far off now, bobbed once more above the green and was gone home.

It knew. The whole town knew. Sundays.

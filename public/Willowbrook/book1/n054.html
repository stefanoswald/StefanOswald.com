Night 54

Two Shots and a Lollipop for Later

Month 2 — Smiles and Sleep

The two-month checkup had a scale, a tape measure, a doctor with warm hands — and, at the end, the part every parent dreads more than the baby does.

Vaccines. The little thigh, the quick sting, the enormous betrayed wail. Sixty full seconds of heartbreak.

And then — this is the part nobody believes until they see it — it was over. Nursing started, the wail wound down through gears of complaint into snuffles, into rhythm, into none. By the parking lot, Olivia was asleep. By dinnertime she had forgotten the entire government.

What she kept was invisible and permanent: her immune system, that eager student, now holding wanted posters for some of the oldest enemies of babies — the coughs and fevers that once walked through towns like Willowbrook taking children as they went. Grandpa Joe could name them from his own mother's stories. That is why he called vaccine day, without any joke in his voice, a harvest day.

The evening asked only gentleness: extra snuggles and feeds, expected fussing, maybe a warm little leg where the medicine sat, maybe a slight fever doing its practicing. The doctor's list said when to call; the list stayed quiet.

"Braver than me," Mama told her, which was true.

Olivia slept that night the deep sleep of the immunized, one small bandage like a medal on her thigh.

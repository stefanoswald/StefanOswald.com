Night 52

The Bottle Learns Its Manners

Month 2 — Smiles and Sleep

The bottle joined the family the way all good newcomers do: slowly, and on the baby's terms.

Whatever was in it — Mama's pumped milk some evenings, formula for other families, both being love in liquid form — the manner of it mattered more than parents are usually told. Papa learned paced feeding, which is bottle-speak for let the baby drive. Olivia held snug and half-upright, never flat. The bottle held gently level, so the milk arrived by her effort, not gravity's. And pauses — tipping the nipple down every so often, letting her breathe and look about and decide, genuinely decide, whether she wanted more.

Because bottles, unpaced, pour faster than babies vote. The paced way keeps her own full-o-meter in charge, spares the tummy the too-much-too-fast ache, and turns a feed from a filling station into what it actually is: twenty minutes of being held by someone whose whole job is noticing you.

Fussing at the first bottles is standard; babies are traditionalists. The classic fix is the strangest one: it goes better when Mama is out of the room entirely. Traditions bend faster with the founder off-site.

Olivia drained her evening bottle at her own speed, supervising Papa throughout.

He passed. She burped. The committee adjourned.

Night 241

The Quiet Ten Minutes

Month 8: The Velcro Weeks and the Thaw

This page schedules nothing, teaches nothing, and marks no milestone. It is about ten minutes at the day's end, and it may be, of the year's three hundred and sixty-five, the one the book most hopes gets kept.

The scene, as the almanac found it on an ordinary Thursday. Dinner done, bath not yet. The floor curriculum running itself: the citizen at the rotation basket, deep in the stacking rings' current research, self-employed, requiring nothing. And Mama, this is the entire event, sat down on the floor against the couch, three feet off, and did not do anything.

Not the phone. Not the folding. Not the narration, even, the documentary's voice, faithful since month two, resting. Not the next thing. Ten minutes of sitting in the same room as her child's absorbed and independent play, watching it the way you watch weather you love: present, unrequired, off the clock.

The page defends the practice with everything it has, because the current of the age runs hard the other way. The parenting year is a doing year, the book itself is four hundred doings deep, the feeding and proofing and naming and audit upon audit, and the doing is the job and the job is real. But the doing acquires its own momentum, and somewhere in it, unwatched, the being-with gets rescheduled into the gaps, and the gaps fill, and a parent can spend a whole devoted day beside a child and never once be simply with her, off-duty, unproductive, there.

The ten minutes are the correction, and their findings, filed from the couch-side floor. That the play deepens when unassisted, the old yes-space law, working on the parent now: unhovered, she worked the rings through four full cycles, narrating to herself in chains, the self-employment the whole doing-year had built running at capacity precisely because nobody helped. That the watching, undiluted, banks what the camera roll cannot, the exact dusk-lit fact of her at eight months and change, the concentration frown, the one sock gone, the ring held up at last not for assistance but for company: shown, per the gift economy, to the watcher, because the watcher was there to show it to.

And that the being-with is not the doing's opposite but its point, the thing all the doings were ever for, available at zero cost, three feet off, ten minutes at a time, going by at the speed of the whole first year: once, and not again, and worth attending.

The ring was shown. The showing was received.

Nothing else happened. It was, the almanac's entry runs entire, everything.

Sit down, the page counsels every reader, tonight if the floor allows. Ten minutes. The dusk manages itself. The year is going by right there on the rug, and it is, the book gives its word, worth watching.

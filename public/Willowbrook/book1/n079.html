Night 79

Dinner With Both Hands

Month 3: Rhythm

A quiet revolution came to dinnertime: Olivia joined the table.

Not to eat, the milk months had months to run. She came as a guest, parked in her bouncer on a chair-free corner where she could see faces, and the effect surprised everyone. The fussing that used to summon a standing, swaying parent away from cooling plates eased off. She had not wanted milk at six o'clock, it turned out. She had wanted in.

Babies are guests like that: what they crave at the day's social hour is the society, faces trading talk, the music of table conversation, her name arriving in sentences like a small gift each time. So the family began the habit years early, the one the research quietly cheers for: eat together, with the baby present, from the start. She learns the rhythms of conversation months before words. The parents eat warm food with both hands, a luxury of empires.

One more table trick rode along: narrating the meal. "Papa is having the potatoes. Mama is having seconds of the potatoes." Vocabulary, served nightly.

Olivia followed the talk like a very short diplomat, contributing one qualified "ah-goo" during a lull, unanimously carried.

Dinner, population three, all present.

The potatoes, for the record, were excellent. Both hands confirmed it.

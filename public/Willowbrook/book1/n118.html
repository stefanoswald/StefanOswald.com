Night 118

Three Toys at a Time

Month 4: Rolling and Laughing

The toys had multiplied, the way toys do, gifts, hand-me-downs, impulse rattles, until the play mat resembled a small prosperous bazaar, and Olivia, set down amid the inventory, played with none of it.

She batted one thing, dropped it for the next, surfed the surfaces of six toys in four minutes, and fussed, a shopper overwhelmed in her own store.

So the house tried the librarian's trick, old as toy chests: the rotation. Three toys on the mat. Three. The wooden ring, the crinkle block, the standing mirror. Everything else into the basket, the basket onto the shelf, the shelf out of sight.

The change arrived the same morning. With the bazaar reduced to a gallery, Olivia settled on the ring and worked it, turned it, mouthed it, passed it hand to hand across the border, dropped it, retrieved the plan, resumed. Eleven minutes on one object, the fourth month's version of a doctoral seminar. Fewer choices had bought deeper attention, exactly as the research keeps finding and grandmothers keep already knowing.

The rotation's rhythm: swap the gallery every few days, and old toys return as new ones, the crinkle block, restored after a week's exile, was received like a returning ship. Novelty, manufactured from nothing, on a rotating schedule, free.

The bazaar stayed in the basket, appreciating.

The scholar stayed on her mat, absorbed, one ring from a full professorship.

Less, it turned out, was more. It usually turns out that way, in shops and childhoods both.

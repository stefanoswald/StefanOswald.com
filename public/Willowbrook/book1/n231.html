Night 231

The First Shoes

Month 8 — The Velcro Weeks and the Thaw

The season's warming pavements raised, on schedule, the question the barefoot doctrine had deferred all winter: the outdoors now hosted a citizen who cruised its benches and would shortly walk its ground — and the ground, unlike the living room, kept gravel.

The expedition to the shoe shop was therefore mounted, and the page rides along with the doctrine's field guide.

The doctrine itself, restated at the shop door for every household making the trip: shoes are for surfaces, not for skills. Indoors, barefoot remained the standing law of the balance curriculum — the soles reading the floor being the vestibular department's primary text — and no shoe, however dear, improves upon skin at the trade of learning to walk. The outdoor pair exists for protection: pavement, splinters, the customs of establishments. It is equipment, not therapy.

The specification, delivered to the shop's old fitter, who knew it before it was spoken: soft-soled — flexible enough to fold nearly in half, the foot's own mechanics unimpeded, the thin barrier and nothing more. Lightweight. Roomy at the toes by the thumb's-width standard, growth being the only guarantee in the file. And fastened to survive the era's second business, because the first business of all first shoes, universally, is their removal — the almanac would log the inaugural pair's first field trip as three wearings, eleven removals, one shoe lost at the park and recovered from the stroller's undercarriage at bath time.

The fitting itself ran the classic theater: the feet measured on the sliding gauge (a device unchanged in a century, and correct), the first pair installed upon feet that regarded them as a category error, the test cruise conducted along the shop's bench with the gait of a small astronaut renegotiating gravity — and the fitter's verdict, delivered over spectacles with the authority of ten thousand such fittings: "She'll forget them in a week. They all do."

The page appends the fitter's other counsel, gratis, as the shop's bell rang them out: check the fit monthly — the first year of shoes is four sizes for some feet — and buy for today's foot, never tomorrow's; the growing room beyond a thumb's width is a stumble subsidy.

The inaugural pair rode home in their box, were removed twice en route from feet they had never been on, and took their place by the door beside the household's larger fleet.

Small equipment, for the small ground ahead.

The ground, gravel and all, was coming. The pavement, warming daily, could wait a few more weeks for its customer. The customer, barefoot at the window per doctrine, pointed at her own shoes on the mat and pronounced upon them her entire relevant vocabulary:

"Uh-oh."

Accurate, ruled the almanac, as usual. Filed.

Night 217

Five A.M., Newly Permanent

Month 8 — The Velcro Weeks and the Thaw

The night sleep, renovated and settled since the fourth month's storm, developed in the eighth a new architectural feature, and the feature was five a.m.

Not a wake-up in the old sense — no cry, no case to answer. A start time. The monitor delivered it daily now: the rustle, the rail-haul to standing, and the morning address commencing in the dark like a rooster who had done graduate work. Wide awake. Fully charged. Ready for the cupboard, the blocks, the entire docket — at an hour the household's other two citizens considered, on the record, to be night.

The page runs the era's diagnostics in the order the house ran them. The bedtime audit, first suspect as ever: too early a bedtime banks a full tank by five (the treaty's hours were checked and held true — no adjustment owed). The anchor nap's health: confirmed robust; cutting day sleep to buy morning sleep, the doctrine warns and the trial confirmed, buys only an overtired five a.m. instead. The room: the thaw's earlier light was found guilty on one count — the curtains upgraded to genuine blackout, worth twenty minutes on their own. The silence protocol: entries before six kept dim and dull per the old boring-night rules, milk offered without festivities, the message held steady — this hour is night, whatever your administration has ruled.

And then the era's true doctrine, the one the veterans at the bench deliver with their flattest smiles: some of it is simply the age, and some of it is simply her. Early waking waves through the eight-to-ten-month country on the skill storms and the light's turning, and recedes, mostly, on its own schedule — and underneath the wave, every baby carries a factory setting, and hers, all evidence suggested, was dawn.

The household adapted per its oldest skill: the shifts. Five a.m. became a rotation — one parent down with the rooster and the coffee, one banking sleep till seven — and the dawn shift, once accepted as a shift rather than fought as a malfunction, revealed its own smuggled goods: the house at first light, one small person at maximum charm and zero competition, the day's first cupboard summit convened while the street still slept.

"You're a lark," Papa told the chairwoman at 5:15, over blocks.

"Ba!" confirmed the lark, gaveling.

The coffee was strong. The light, a little earlier each week now, came up on the two of them mid-session, and was pointed at.

Night 248

Pavement Day

Month 9: The Edge of Everything

The first shoes, benched by doctrine since the fitting, entered field service on the month's finest Saturday: pavement day, the walking taken public, the town's actual ground brought underfoot at last.

The debut, conducted per the fleet manual. The venue: the front path, swept, sun-warmed, graded flat, the gentlest public surface on the household's map. The rig: the soft soles laced to specification, checked for the month's growth per the fitter's counsel (holding, barely; the second pair already forecast). The escort: one paternal hand held at her preferred altitude, the hand-held walking being its own gait on the two-gait ledger, cheaper than solo and good for triple the range.

And the walk itself: the path's whole length, eleven yards, the longest upright journey of her career, conducted at the pace the new ground demanded, because the pavement, the debut confirmed at the first stride, was a different country entire. The living room's floors were known territory, read barefoot per doctrine down to the last rug seam; the path arrived through the soles' new diplomacy as texture upon texture, the concrete's grit, the expansion joints (each one halted at, studied, stepped over with both feet and full ceremony), one pebble (archaeological pace engaged, per the pincer forecast; the specimen processed and pocketed by the escort ahead of customs), and at the path's end, the great prize the whole franchise had waited for: grass, walked on, in shoes, the lawn crossed at a run, the almanac certifies the word; a run, four strides of it, ending in the era's finest documented landing.

The page marks the day's true cargo under its comedy. The world's ground, the pavement and the grass, the gravel and the mud season's legacy puddles (one located, per destiny; the boots' franchise opening early), is the walking's real curriculum, every surface a new invoice for the balance ledger, the outdoor walker compiling in weeks what the living room could never bill. The escort hand, offered always and imposed never, spending down naturally as the invoices cleared. And the town itself receiving its newest pedestrian per its oldest custom: the porch waves returned now from her own altitude, the neighbor's old gold dog saluted eye-to-eye at the fence line, Willowbrook's sidewalks, the ones this book has strolled since the ninth day of her life, hosting at last the citizen they'd been rolling toward all year.

The return trip ran half-walked, half-freighted on the shoulder line, one shoe removed per tradition and recovered per protocol.

Pavement: achieved. Grass: run upon. Pebble: confiscated.

The town's ground, underfoot at last, held steady, the way it would for every mile she'd ever walk on it.

The path's eleven yards went in the ledger beside the living room's first three feet.

Same walker. Bigger map. The book's whole geography, opening.

Night 195

The Video Call Grandfather

Month 7 — Explorers and Guardrails

The snow week suspended the Sunday drive, and the farm arrived instead by screen: Grandpa Joe in his kitchen, straw hat indoors as ever, filling the propped tablet like weather.

The page pauses first on its own fine print, since screens are this book's most-asked question at the bench and the porch: the standing counsel keeps the first years essentially screenless — the research on passive watching earning its warnings — with one exception written into every guideline that ever ruled on it: the video call. The call is not television. It is serve-and-return down a wire — a face that answers, names aimed, pauses held for her return serve — the whole social curriculum, merely transmitted. The exception exists because the grandparents exist, and the science, for once, sided fully with the heart.

The call itself ran eleven minutes of certified curriculum. Recognition, first — the study, the lookback to Mama (is this weather real?), then the bloom: the arm-pump, the ma-ma-adjacent syllable the family had begun suspecting meant something like known person, delight. The wave exchanged, his slow farm version and her windshield-wiper, mutually admired. The pat — flat palm on his flannel chest, the touching-the-screen phase every family logs, half greeting, half science. And the old man running his fence-line liturgy remotely: the chickens carried to the lens one by one (Henrietta, unimpressed by broadcast; the Duchess declining to appear, her situation ongoing), each bird named into the beam, the naming game playing at forty miles' range.

"Well now," he said at the close, which the whole family could now translate: seen you. Good. Sundays soon.

The call's last frame was the two waves going at once, different grammars, same sentence.

The tablet went dark and was patted twice more, hopefully, then released.

The page's counsel, closing: build the call into the winter's structure — same evenings, short and often beating long and rare, the remote faces kept current in the small files. Distance is a fact of families. The wire, used this one way, is where the fact goes to lose.

Sunday resumed by carriage the following week, the screen's understudy role complete.

The pat, however, transferred: Grandpa Joe, in person, was patted on the chest at arrival, twice, flat-palmed.

Broadcast confirmed live, said the gesture. The farm rang with it.

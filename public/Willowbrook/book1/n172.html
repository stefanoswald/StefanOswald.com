Night 172

The Restaurant Trial

Month 6: The Feast Begins

Six months of kitchen-table date nights had banked the courage, and Friday the family went public: the corner place on the square, five-thirty sharp, party of three, one of whom would be dining from a bag.

The campaign ran on doctrine assembled from Rosa's briefings and the almanac's travel pages, filed here for every household staging the same trial.

Timing, first and decisive: the early seating, restaurant dawn, rooms half-empty, kitchens fast, the diner's own witching hour still two hours off. The corner booth requisitioned, stroller-parkable, blast-radius contained. The kit: bib, spoon, the puff arsenal, one silent toy, and the veteran's masterstroke, order everything at once, appetizers through check, the whole meal compressed toward the fuse.

The trial itself ran twenty-nine minutes of documented success. The bread crust, issued by a waiter of evident fatherhood, was gnawed through two courses like a season ticket. The puffs deployed at the entrée in a slow scatter, each one a minute of pincer-priced silence. The state dined hot, both-handed, in public, remarking twice on the miracle in real time. At minute thirty the fuse announced itself, the arch, the first rising note, and the doctrine's final clause executed without appeal: pay fast, pack calm, exit waving. Never bargain with the fuse.

Ice cream on the walk home, the trial's traditional medal, shared in microscopic spoonfuls with a first-timer who voted with her entire face.

The page's finding: restaurants with babies are not the old restaurants, and grading them so ruins both. Graded as sorties, early, brief, corner-boothed, exit-ready, they return actual civilization at sustainable cost. The trick is not making the baby fit the old evening. It is building the new evening baby-shaped, and eating it hot.

Party of three, the record shows. Repeat business likely.

The corner place kept the crust program running for years. Establishments of quality always do.

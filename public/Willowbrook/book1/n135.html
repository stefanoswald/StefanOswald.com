Night 135

The Small Hours Return

Month 5 — Nearly Sitting, Nearly Everything

The nights had been civilized for weeks — and then, this week, the small hours reopened for business.

Four a.m., mostly. A cry with no story to it: not hunger's rhythm, not pain's alarm — just surfaced, and finding the surfacing lonely. The fifth month runs a busy calendar of causes: the sitting practice firing through sleeping muscles (skills rehearse at night, loudly), the teething foothills, a growth gathering, or the sleep architecture simply auditing its own cycles.

The house met it with the settled doctrine, refined across five months and worth restating on one page.

First: the pause. A minute of listening before moving — surfacings often resolve themselves, and a baby mid-resettle rescued too fast is a resettle undone. Then, if the cry gathered instead of faded: the low-lamp visit, the hand on the chest, the shush from the doorway of her sleep rather than the center of it — comfort at the minimum effective dose, the boring-night rules kept holy. The feed held in reserve, offered when the cry said feed, not merely said something.

And between visits, the older doctrine, for the parents: no arithmetic at four a.m. No forecasting the ruin of all future sleep from one loud week. The week is a week. They end. Five months had proven it four times.

Tonight took two visits and one held-in-reserve feed, spent at 4:40 on the evidence.

By morning, no one remembered the case details. The file, like all the night's files, closed itself.

The small hours would keep occasional business hours for years. The doctrine was the doctrine. It held, at the minimum effective dose, every time.

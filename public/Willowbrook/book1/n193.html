Night 193

The Wave

Month 7: Explorers and Guardrails

The gesture curriculum, running since the sign for more, graduated its social wing this month: the wave.

It had been modeled at her, per custom, a thousand times, every departure and porch pass, bye-bye with the flapping hand, one of the era's unkillable parental reflexes. The first returns came in the strange mirror-grammar of all first waves: fingers opening and closing at her own face, palm inward, waving, the texts note fondly, at herself, the gesture copied exactly as seen from the receiving end. The correction shipped on its own within weeks, as it always does, unlectured.

And then the deployment, aimed and social and timed: at the front window, frost at the glass edges, Papa's car backing down the drive, the whole arm going, windshield-wiper style, bye-bye landing on its true target for the first time. The car horn answered, two taps. The wave doubled.

The page files the wave with the point and the gift in the seventh month's great social flowering, and notes what distinguishes it: the wave is pure ritual, no object moves, nothing is acquired; it is meaning alone, performed because the tribe performs it, her first full citizenship gesture in the ceremony layer of human life. (Clapping arrived the same fortnight, and so-big by December, the whole gesture wardrobe assembling in its usual burst once the first piece lands.)

Its daily wages, though, were paid at the doors. The wave gave the goodbyes a job: something for the hands to do while the feelings ran, the ritual holding the shape, leaving is half of a shape, in muscle now, not just experience. The door-problem months had built the bridge; the wave was the flag on it. Departures with a wave in them, the almanac logged plainly, ran measurably easier than the waveless kind, for all parties, including the party in the car glancing at the mirror.

Bye-bye. Two horn taps. The frost, the window, the arm going.

Then breakfast, on schedule, the wave's other lesson, the one it would teach at every door for years: after the goodbye, the day simply continues, and the wavers in it.

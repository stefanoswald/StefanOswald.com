Night 139

Six Months of Marriage Later

Month 5 — Nearly Sitting, Nearly Everything

The anniversary of nothing arrived on a Thursday — no date on it, just the evening the dishes got ignored and the kitchen got danced in.

It had been five months and change since the door on Maple Lane opened for three. This page takes the couple's census, as the book does quarterly for the baby, because the marriage is infrastructure too, and infrastructure gets inspected.

The findings, honestly filed. They were tireder than any prior era, and kinder in it — the 4 a.m. handoffs had built a reflex of mercy that was outlasting the 4 a.m.s. They spoke a new dialect, half logistics ("she's fed, bath's run, your window's now") and half code (the eyebrow, the Maybe, the two-finger tap that meant sit down, I've got this). The old dialect — the one about books and plans and nonsense — had thinned, and they had caught it thinning, which is the whole battle: the date nights at the kitchen table, the twenty-minute no-baby-talk rule, the walk taken together while Rosa held the fort one traded morning.

The counsel this page files under couple maintenance: small and often beats grand and never. The six-second kiss in the kitchen. The thanks said out loud for the invisible shifts. The argument had early, small, before it compounds. The dance, unscheduled, to no music, mid-mess.

The monitor stayed quiet the whole song.

"We should do this weekly," said Papa, turning her past the dishwasher.

"Maybe," said Mama, cheek to his shoulder — meaning, in the old dialect, which they both still spoke after all: yes.

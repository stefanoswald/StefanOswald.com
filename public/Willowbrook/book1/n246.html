Night 246

The Two Gaits

Month 9: The Edge of Everything

The month after the step ran on a two-gait economy, and the page documents it against the era's most common parental misreading.

The walking: the prestige line. Three steps, five, a wobbling nine at the week's record, deployed for occasions: the crossing to opened arms, the traverse between couch and wagon, the show-the-grandfather demonstration performed on the Tuesday call at full ceremony. Upright travel was expensive, every stride a balance invoice, and the operator, a sound economist, spent it where it paid.

The crawling: the freight line, retained at full schedule. The kitchen commute, the hallway long-haul, the anywhere-fast of a citizen with cargo or deadlines, all of it ran on the old four-beat gait at the old velocity, and the almanac logged the era's characteristic sight daily: the walker taking her three proud steps, dropping to all fours mid-room like a commuter changing trains, and freighting the remaining distance at twice the speed.

The misreading, retired here for every household mid-transition: the drop to crawling is not regression, not laziness, not the walking failing to take. It is the transition's own architecture, every new gait in the human catalog runs beside its predecessor while the invoices come down, the crawl surrendering routes to the walk one corridor at a time over weeks (the almanac's own log: the couch-to-wagon line went upright in week one; the kitchen commute held freight until nearly the month's end; the hallway, last and longest, fell to walking only when the walking got cheap). Pushing the transition, standing her up off her own chosen crawl, the well-meant hauling, buys nothing and taxes the economist's sound judgment; the routes convert on their own arithmetic, per the whole book's respect for the operator's almanac.

And the era's secret, entered fondly: the two-gait weeks are brief and never come again, and they are the year's best theater, the train-changing mid-room, the freight runs arriving with prestige finishes (the last three feet to the cupboard taken upright, for the door's own dignity), the daily redrawing of the household's map by a surveyor working in two elevations.

By the month's close the invoices had collapsed: the walking cheap, the routes converted, the freight line running its last scheduled service on the stairs (where it would hold its franchise, per the academy's backward-down law, for a long time yet).

The crawl's retirement, when it came, went unmarked, the almanac catching it only in arrears, the way the first year's eras always closed: noticed gone, mourned a little, filed.

Two gaits, one economist, every route hers now at every elevation.

The map, redrawn again. The surveyor, upright, already sighting the next country.

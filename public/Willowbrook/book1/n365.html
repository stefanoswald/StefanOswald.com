Night 365

The Bear Moves In

Month 12 — The Candle's Country

The guests went home at the blessed early hour. The house returned to its three. The liturgy ran its four eternal steps one last first-year time — the bath's frosting-archaeology, the pajamas, the book, the song — and at the crib, at the year's own final station, the promise the whole book had kept was kept:

The bear moved in.

Papa lifted him from the dresser — the post held three hundred and sixty-five documented nights, the division's whole correspondence complete — and placed him, per the twelve-month statutes' own lifted embargo, in the crib: at the corner's term, at the sleeper's own reach, the flopped ear at its destined altitude, the re-stitched heart at attention, the watch's oldest member at his commission's true address at last.

The one-year-old, at the drowsy accord's edge, turned. Found him. Gathered him in at both certified arms — the grip his letters had studied all year, arrived — and slept, per the almanac's final boxed entry, exactly as forecast: all in, holding everything, even sleeping.

"Nigh-nigh, Baba," at the settling register, aimed, at the year's last documented coin.

The dresser stood empty at the moonlight's term. It had never, the page certifies, looked more decorated.

And the watch assembled, per the town's whole liturgy, at the window's gold. Stella at the sill's licensed protocol, low and warm. The Sleepy Moon at its deepest documented hum. The willow's gold letting down on the hill where a word lived in the rings; the brook carrying the day's file downstream where it had always known it first; the crickets' winter successors at the ledger's last line; one balloon at the far pasture's anchor, home; and at the willow's oldest branch, a round elderly regard at the spectacles' own angle, asking — per his office, at the shelf's whole turning — the year's last question, which was, as ever, the blessing:

"And what will they read tomorrow?"

"The next one," said Stella, at the watch's own certainty. "From One to Two. It's on the shelf. I've read ahead — I told her at the sill. There's a grocery store in it, and a girl named Lily, and a Maybe that turns into the whole rest of her life. There are six more books, old friend. There's a whole shelf. We have the watch for all of it."

"Mmmmm," hummed the Moon — which is Moon, the whole town could translate, for then it isn't an ending at all; it never was; blow out the candle and the light just moves to the next page; it's the oldest arrangement there is; I've held it since the first bedtime ever kept.

Stella twinkled once, for yes — for the whole year of yes.

Below, at the small correct darkness, the first year's last page held its term: the crib, the bear, the sleeper, the rise and the fall, the rise and the fall — the tide, one year old, still the tide, at four counts a minute's parental arithmetic, at the doorway's last extra minute, at the whole town's assembled gold.

Three hundred and sixty-five nights down in the town of Willowbrook. The candle: kept. The bear: home. The book: complete.

The shelf: just beginning.

Goodnight, Olivia. Goodnight, walker, talker, wonder-watcher, one-year-old.

Goodnight, Mama. Goodnight, Papa. You did it. You did the whole year. The book gives its word, at its own last line: you did it beautifully.

Goodnight, Willowbrook.

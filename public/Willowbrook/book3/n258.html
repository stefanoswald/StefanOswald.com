Night 258

The Fair Share Problem

Month 9: Counting to Five

Thursday. Snack. And

mathematics arrived

at its first hard

problem:

FIVE berries.

TWO friends.

The division, attempted:

"One for you, one for

me."

(Two gone. Three left.)

"One for you, one for

me."

(Four gone. One left.)

One berry.

Two girls.

The oldest remainder

problem in the world,

sitting red and round

on the plate.

The old solutions,

considered:

Grab it? (The mine era.

Retired. Mostly.)

Biggest gets it? (Same

size. Dead end.)

Rock-paper-scissors?

(Neither knows it yet.

The far books hold it.)

And then, from the

deep treaty archives,

the cookie precedent,

the whole friendship's

case law...

Lily: "SPLIT it."

The berry, bitten

carefully in half by

the guest (guest bites,

host chooses, a protocol

they invented on the

spot, so fair it should

be in constitutions),

and the halves compared,

and the host choosing...

and both halves eaten

in ceremonial unison.

Five berries. Two friends.

Two-and-a-half each.

"Two and a HALF,"

repeated Olivia,

tasting the number.

(A HALF. Fractions,

arriving the only way

they ever truly arrive:

through dessert.)

The remainder problem:

solved.

The treaty: extended

to all future remainders.

Mathematics and friendship,

the month's finding:

same tool kit.

Count fair. Split the

last one.

Everything else is

commentary.

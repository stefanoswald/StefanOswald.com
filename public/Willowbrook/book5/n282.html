Night 282

The Unfair Loss

Month 10: Losing Well

This month's hardest chapter arrived at kickball: she was safe. Clearly. Visibly. Three witnesses safe. And the call, made by the recess monitor, distracted, mid-whistle, went out. Game over. Her run: erased. The other team: cheering a win they didn't quite earn.

The protest: made, correctly, calmly ("'scuse me, I was safe, ask the fence kids"). The call: stood anyway. (Monitors' calls stand. The way things worked says. The way things worked, this once, was wrong.) And the lamp got the real question, the one the whole month was building toward: "The dice have no memory, fine.

But the call had a face, Mama. A person got it wrong and the wrongness stood and there's no rematch for a stood call. How do you lose well when the losing wasn't true?" Mama took it whole: "That's the graduate course, love, and here's all I know at forty: you say your truth once, clearly, you did.

You let the story of witnesses hold it, it does; the fence kids know; you know. And then you put it down. Not because it was fair. Because carrying a stood call forever costs you, not the call.

Some losses are wrong and stay wrong. You don't have to call them right. You just have to not build your house on the grudge... Say it once. Let the witnesses hold it. Put it down. Don't build on the grudge."

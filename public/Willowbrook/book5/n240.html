Night 240

The Solstice Kept Again

Month 8: The Settling In

The solstice came round again, and this year, by family appointment, she ran the ceremony. (The traditions pass down while the passers are still there to watch. This family does it on purpose.) the routine, as done by its five-year-old-in-nineteen-days birthday girl: the candles at the window, lit by her hand. The cocoa, distributed.

And the teaching, delivered to Theo, who stood exactly where she'd stood two winters back, at the same window, the same age-ish, the same wide eyes: "Fee-oh. Tonight's the longest dark of the whole year. The most night there is. And here's the secret that the whole town's candles are saying: Tonight's the turning.

Tomorrow: a little more light. Every day after: more. The darkest night is the one where the light starts coming back. The year does its Maybe night tonight, the worst dark is secretly the turnaround, and we light the candles to say: We know.

We remember. We're not scared of you, dark. You're the doorway, not the wall." Theo, absorbing, breath fogging the window: "... The dark is the doorway. The dark is the doorway. Now drink your cocoa. The light's already coming while we stand here.

That's the whole magic: it turns without us, but watching it turn, together, every year, that part's ours."

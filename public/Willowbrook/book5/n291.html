Night 291

The Sibling Race Handicap, Revisited

Month 10: Losing Well

The corner footrace (standing institution: lamppost to mailbox, best of three, Theo's head start recalibrated quarterly by the fairness engineering) had its spring adjustment this week, and produced this month's quietest big moment: The data: Theo's stride, measured on the sidewalk squares: Longer. His head start: shrunk from five squares to three. "He's gaining, Papa. The gap closes a little every quarter.

The math says..." She ran it, twice. "someday the handicap hits zero. And someday after that... He outruns you. He outruns me." The look down the lane, at the mailbox, at the whole arithmetic of little brothers.

"And the losing school says: that day's not a loss. That day is the program working. The whole point of the catcher at the slide bottom and the letters and the checkers trap and the head starts was... This.

Him, gaining. You don't teach somebody to fly and then get mad at the altitude." (the story, from far downstream: the zero-handicap day came in book seven. She called the race herself, fair, from the start line. He won by half a stride, and the first thing he did at the mailbox was turn around, the catcher's reflex, inherited whole. Nobody lost anything that day. The program worked.)

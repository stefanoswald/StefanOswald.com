Night 278

The Race She Trained For

Month 10: Losing Well

The spring fun run, the kids' quarter mile, the one she trained for. Three weeks of route-runs with Papa. The handicap-race engineering, this time aimed at her own legs. She wanted this one.

Publicly. Out loud. Second place. By a full stride. To a kid who trained harder, ran cleaner, deserved it whole. The hot thing's biggest test of this month, because this loss had investment in it.

Board games are rented; this one was owned. And at the finish line, chest heaving, medal-less, the program produced its graduation move: she crossed to the winner, before the hot thing could veto, stuck out her hand, and said the true thing: "You ran it clean. The last stretch, when I pushed, you had more. That's training I didn't do.

Good race." And then she cried anyway, into Mama's coat, thirty seconds, complete. (Both, the story insists. The handshake and the tears. Two coats. Losing well was never not-feeling. Losing well is feeling in the right order: honor first, grief second, both whole.)

"The sting means I wanted it..." She said at the lamp. "Wanting things is the whole engine. If losing stopped stinging, it'd mean I stopped wanting. Keep the sting. Just... put it second."

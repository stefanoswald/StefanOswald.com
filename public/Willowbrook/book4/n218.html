Night 218

The Casserole Route

Month 8: Helping Hands

The casserole economy... the town's oldest current, picked up volume this month: June's mama: New baby. (The blanket delivered last fall; the baby arrived this month; the meals rota activated. The family's slot: Tuesdays.) Grandpa: The hip.

("It's nothing," he said. It was something. The town fed him through it, over his objections, which is the town's whole specialty.) Miss Dana: The flu. (A week out. The pickup-line parliament organized within hours.

Twelve families, seven days, covered.) And the delivery crew, wagon-powered, every run: Route skills, compounding: The knock-and-step-back. ("New babies sleep. Sick people shuffle. You knock and you wait and you don't ring twice.")

The no-lingering rule. ("Drop da food, say da nice thing, go. Sick houses don't need hosting-energy. Da food is da visit.") (The duck as provenance mark. The town learned to read it. A ducked dish meant the Oswald wagon.

It meant: loved. The far books show ducked dishes crisscrossing that lane for twenty years.) "Why do we feed everybody?" asked Theo, hauling. And his sister, without breaking stride: "Because everybody fed us. Da basket came before you were born.

Twice. We're not giving, Fee-oh. We're giving back. It just never stops being our turn." It never stops being anyone's turn. That's the whole town.

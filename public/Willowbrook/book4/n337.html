Night 337

The Doorframe Mark

Month 12: Almost Four

The doorframe. (Kitchen. The one with the lines. The family's whole vertical history, penciled: every birthday, every name, climbing the wood year over year.) The ceremony, fourth annual: Shoes: off. Heels: flat to the frame.

("No tiptoes. Da frame knows. Da frame always knows.") The book on the head. The pencil. The line... and the step back, to see: "... Dat much?! From dere to dere?! Dat's a whole hand of growing!

I grew a hand dis year!" (A hand-width. Sure enough: big year. They don't come bigger. The frame now reads, bottom to top: the baby line... Papa holding her flat against the wood, age one; the wobbly two; the proud three; and now this... the fourth line, a hand above, with the year's whole lesson somehow inside the gap: the meadow, the letters, the deep end, the green room.

Growing is invisible daily and enormous annually. The frame's whole lesson.) Then: "Fee-oh's turn," the apprentice, marked, delighted, his second line ever, already a thumb above his first... and the comparison, inevitable: "He's catching up!

You're both growing. Dat's not catching. Dat's parade." And last, quietly, at her own new line, one finger on the pencil mark: "Hi, Four- line. Da udders are all people I used to be... I keep dem doh.

Da frame keeps dem for me." The frame keeps them for you. Every one. That's what frames are for. (Ask the far pages what became of that doorframe. Some wood is archive. This family knows which.)

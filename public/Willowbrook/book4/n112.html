Night 112

The Morning After

Month 4: The Dark Is Just the Day Resting

Morning at the farm. The survivor of the solo night, at the big table, retelling it already: "...and da dark was different but I gots my gold light and Baba worked da whole shift and den Gampa showed me da full dark, da star kind, and I went back to sleep all by myself and da roosters did da waking..." (Twice, the retelling.

Pancakes throughout. Grandpa nodding at every part like sworn telling, which it was.) The pickup, mid- morning: the car, the parents, and the run... the full-speed, whole-body, feet- off-the-ground homecoming hug.

(Both directions. The parents' night, as it happened, had its own dark to manage, the first-ever without her under their roof. They used their own tools. The house had felt enormous. They'd checked her empty room twice.

Solo sleepovers are a two-way drill. Everyone passed.) The debrief, in the car: "Would you do it again? Next weekend." (Negotiated to: next month. Standing date. The farm sleepover became an institution... monthly, then whenever, then, in the far books, whole summer weeks.

The anytime, fully cashed at last, paying out for years.) And Grandpa, at the truck, at the goodbye, to the parents, quietly, over her head: "She checked on me, y'know. Two a.m. Heard my chair creak and came to see if I was okay." (...

She checked on him. The night kit's undocumented tool: the checking goes both ways now. The keeper's instinct, portable, even at three, even at midnight, even away.) "Well now..." He finished, at the moment. "She'll do." She'll do. Highest words the farm gives. Earned overnight. Kept forever.

Night 136

The Hand-Me-Down Ceremony

Month 5: Little Brother Tags Along

A ceremony this week, invented by its officiant: The handing down. (The trike is outgrown. A bigger bike... training wheels, Mateo-blessed... arrived for her birthday-coming. And the trike... the trike goes downhill. Like the flip.

Like everything. But not, she ruled, in a pile. Ceremonially.) The conveyances, performed at the porch, formally: The trike: "Fee-oh. Dis trike was Mateo's, den mine. Da hill taught my legs on it. Now it's yours.

Da bell works. Da circle is hard first. I'll teach you da hill trick." (The bell: rung once by the officiant, in farewell. Then by its new owner, eleven times, in office.) The boots: the red ones, outgrown.

"For puddles. Da big pothole knows dese boots. Introduce yourself." The bee costume: wings and all. "You're da bee now. Da buzz comes with it. Practice." And the story's note on the ceremony's invention: hand-me-downs given in piles feel like leftovers.

Handed in ceremonies, they arrive as inheritance... each object trailing its history, its tricks, its previous riders, richer than new. The lane's whole economy, dignified by one three-year- old's instinct for ritual. "Everything I outgrow..."

She declared. "gets a ceremony." Everything did, ever after. The receiving, always at attention. The bell, always rung twice: farewell, and office.

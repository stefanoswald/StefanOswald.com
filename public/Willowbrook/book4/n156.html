Night 156

The Hard Drop-Off

Month 6: Preschool, Turns, and Shares

Day one: triumph. Day two: fine. Day three: the wobble. (On schedule. The books know day three. The shine wears off, the permanence lands... "wait, this is every day?"... and the tears come. Real ones.

At the door. Fingers in Mama's coat.) "I don't want today. I want home today." And the craft... the hardest craft in the parent kit, held: The feelings: named. ("You want home. Day three is the missing day.

It's real.") The goodbye: Short. Sure. Warm. Not extended. (The lingering goodbye, the books are ruthless, feeds the storm. The short one starves it. It feels cruel. It's the kindness. Kiss, wave, rule recited, go.)

And the receiving: Miss Dana's, a professional's, practiced on a decade of day threes: the scoop to the window, the wave-point ("there she goes... she'll pass twice, watch..."), and the pivot: "Now. I have a problem only you can solve. Buttons' water bowl needs filling and you have the steadiest hands in this room..."

(A job. Jobs prevent knocking. The fort law, running in every good classroom on earth.) The tears: dry by water-bowl. The bear: deployed at rest time... his actual job, at last, in the cubby-country: held, by the old letter, by somebody learning that the mamas come back.

The pickup report, from Miss Dana, quiet, to Mama: "Ninety seconds after you left. That's all it was. They're always fine in ninety seconds. It's the parents who take till noon." (It's the parents who take till noon.

Mama took till noon. Everyone passed. Everyone always does. It just costs more some mornings.)

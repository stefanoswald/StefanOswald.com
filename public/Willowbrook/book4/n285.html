Night 285

Grandpa Joe's Reading

Month 10: Words Are Magic

At the farm this week, a discovery, handled with more grace than most grown-ups manage: She asked Grandpa Joe to read the seed catalog aloud. And noticed: he reads slow. Finger under the words.

Lips moving ahead of the sound. The exact method of, she recognized it... Hers. "Grandpa. You read like me." (A silence. The kind where a lesser man deflects. Joe, farmer, mender of everything, deflector of nothing, put down the catalog:) "Letters came hard for me, darlin'.

Still do. School and I quit each other early... farm needed hands more than the county needed another reader, or that's what we told it. I do fine. Your grandma read to me forty years... best hours of my life.

But I'll tell you true: it's the one fence I never fully mended. Don't you leave yours half-built." And her... four, new-lettered, holding the biggest confidence an adult had ever handed her: "... I could read to you.

When I get good. Da almanac. Da catalogs. Whatever you want. Grandma's hours can be my hours now. Some of dem." (Joe turned to the window awhile. Barn dust, he said. Everyone let that stand.)

The arrangement began that season: her, the porch, the catalogs, reading slow... finger under the words, his method, now their method... getting good out loud, for an audience of one old farmer who never missed a session. (Years of it. The far pages hold the last one.

And she reads it still. Finger under the words. On purpose, now. His method. Forever theirs.)

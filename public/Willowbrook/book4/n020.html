Night 20

The Cape Does Nothing

Month 1: Pretend!

A scientific crisis: The cape. The red one. The aunts'. Worn daily for a month. And this week, the test: "If I got a cape... can I fly?" (The question every cape has raised since capes began.)

The experiment, supervised closely: the porch step, the cape deployed, the jump... by the approved both-feet technique... and the landing. On the ground. Where jumps land. The cape: streaming behind. Unhelpful. The answer, delivered with a scientist's honesty: "...

Da cape doesn't fly." A beat of real disappointment. The gap between the world she makes and the world that is... felt, fully, maybe for the first time. And then Papa, sitting down on the step: "Can I tell you what capes actually do?

It's better. Better dan flying? Capes make you braver. Watch: how do you stand when it's on?" (She stood. Chest out. Chin up. Fists on hips. The cape stance. It happens automatically. Nobody can wear a cape slouched.

It's physics of another kind.) "See? The cape doesn't lift you off the ground. It lifts you up inside. That's the real magic. That's why the heroes wear them." She considered her own stance. The lift inside.

Thick enough to touch. "... I keep da cape." She kept the cape. Wore it to the doctor's once. Wore it the first day of preschool, under her jacket, secretly. It never flew. It never needed to. It did the bigger job every single time.

Night 331

The Petersen Fences

Month 11: Grandpa Joe's Almanac

The re-rigging turned real this week: The petersen kids (grown ones; the drought-rotation family's grandchildren; the water went around and now the land does) walked the far fields with Joe, and she attended, fourth-hand official, the almanac under her arm at his request. The walking: boundary by boundary, the old man narrating each field's temperament ("the east slope drains slow, beans hate it; the beans told me, 1983, whole row sulked"; the entries, consulted live; the book working its first day as working seed stock). The Petersens, taking notes like the students they were smart enough to be. And the handshake, at the line fence, the good kind, the kind with fifty years of shared water behind it: "Work it well.

The book's got forty years of its moods, the girl keeps the book; the book makes house calls." ("The land didn't get sold, Mama. It got introduced. To hands the valley already trusts. The fence line where Grandpa and Abel thought is the line the Petersens lease now, the same fence, folded into friendship two generations deep.

The fences don't care. The barns do. And all the barns in this valley been watching each other's smoke for fifty years.")

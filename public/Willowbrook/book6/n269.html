Night 269

The Sorry Grandpa Owed

Month 9: Truth With Kindness

A rare page: Grandpa owed a sorry. (He'd snapped at her, the arthritis morning, the dropped feed bucket, the pain talking loud through him: "just leave it, girl." Sharp. Un-Joe. She'd statue'd, hurt.) And within the hour, the story witnessed the full grown-up edition of the family rule: "Darlin'.

That sharp was mine, not yours. The joints were yelling and I let them use my voice, that's a reason, not a license; you taught me the difference, or your notebook did, which is the same thing. I'm sorry. And the next-time: when the yelling gets loud in me, I'll say 'the joints are loud today' instead of letting them talk.

That's the repair. That's the whole of it." (The lamp got the analysis that night: "He did the whole rule, Mama. The i-did, the next-time, the no-license. Seventy-some years old and the sorry was textbook.

The rule's got no age ceiling, and Mama, it goes the other way too: the grown-ups owing kids sorries and paying them is how kids learn the debts are real. Grandpa paid a six-year-old like the debt was real. Because it was. That's the whole... that's the whole dignity of the whole thing.")

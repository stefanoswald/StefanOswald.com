Night 259

The Apology She Didn't Owe

Month 9: Truth With Kindness

The umpire post delivered this month's subtlest case: a call that a kid's visiting cousin loudly disputed, demanding an apology, escalating, recruiting bystanders, the whole pressure machinery of a person accustomed to winning by volume. The temptation, named honestly at the lamp after: "The sorry would've been cheap, Mama. One word, the yelling stops, the game resumes. Everybody wanted me to pay it.

Even lily's eyes said 'just say it.'" The refusal, as delivered on the court: "I'll say two true things: I'm sorry the call disappoints you, that's real; disappointment's real. And the ball was out, that's real too; the chalk's right there. The first sorry you can have.

The second one doesn't exist, because I'd be apologizing for the truth, and the truth isn't mine to apologize for. It's not even mine. I just called it." (The cousin left. The game resumed anyway.

And the court, the story registers this carefully, trusted her calls more after, not less: "the umpire that won't sell a sorry won't sell a call. The refusing was the resume.")

The Thirsty Month Fundraiser

Month 5: The Brook's Long Story

School brought this month its hardest page: the lesson on water far away. Towns without wells. Kids, kid-kids, her-age kids, walking hours for water the color of the great puddle's day four. The processing, at the lamp, genuinely troubled: "Mama.

I thanked our tap. We walk on a lake. And somewhere a kid my size walks four hours for the bad-color water. The earf shares one cup and some seats at the table get the cup last and that's not the water's fault, the water goes where it goes, it's a plumbing problem.

A second-grid problem. The buried rivers of kindness have plumbing gaps too." The response, inevitable, launched by Friday: The well jar. (Class-adopted. The research done: the real organizations, the real cost of a real well, the number enormous and then, divided by everyone-who-could-give-a-little, suddenly not.)

The mechanism: her specialty, the count public, chalked, rising; the bake sale; the egg-route's given column, redirected for the season. (The jar ran two years, outlasting her class, becoming a school institution. The plaque on the far-away well, when it came, later, reads: "From the kids of Willowbrook. The earf shares one cup.")

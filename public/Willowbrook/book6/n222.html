Night 222

The Fair-Unfair Tally book

Month 8: School Stories

Mr. Okafor, who has learned to hand her class-scale problems ("it's cheaper than fighting it..." He told Ms. Alvarez at the district meeting, the story's spies report), assigned the room's chronic complaint. "It's not fair," to a one-week check: The fair-unfair tally book.

Every "unfair..." Logged, then sorted. The sort, presented Friday: "Column one: Fixable unfairs. (The ball hogging, fixed by rotation. The line-cutting, fixed by the order-of-arrival rule. The calendar collision, fixed by channel.

Thirteen of twenty-two unfairs were fixable. That's the headline: Most unfair is lazy systems, not mean people.) Column two: weather unfairs. (The rain on field day. The flu hitting the play's lead. The dice.

Six of twenty-two: nobody's fault; the kit handles these, you're the sky, etc.) Column three: The real ones. (Three of twenty-two. The deep unfairs, the lunch money kind, the shoe kind, the born-different-decks kind.

These are the hard column. They don't fix with rotations. They fix with whole towns, slow, or they don't fix yet at all. Column three is why the given column exists. It's why the whole second grid exists.

Column three is the grown-up homework, and we're the incoming grown-ups, so: Start the homework now.)"

Night 47

The Ribbon and the Tree

Month 2: The Kite That Wouldn't Fly

The day after: the walk up the hill, alone-ish, the blue ribbon in her pocket. "We won..." She told the tree. "You won. You flew. I know you know; you were in the same wind; the wind that held the kite was the wind in your branches; you touched the whole time."

The gold moved, the way it does. "The judges said 'for the cargo.' The cargo was your words. So the ribbon's yours. That's just the math of it." The ribbon: tucked into the bark's deep fold, beside the gold words.

Home. Family. Growing. Kind. And one blue ribbon, weathering into the tree's keeping, where, sure enough, because the story checks, it stayed. Years. Fading to the gold-grey of old sky. Part of the bark's story now, like the lightning mark, like the initials, like everything the tree wears.

"Two more words coming..." She said, hand on the bark. "I know you know what they are. Craft, the whole craft, you're saving the biggest for last... Fly again next year?" The gold moved. (Yes.

Obviously yes. The kite flew nine more festivals. But that first ribbon stayed in the bark the whole time, and every year the squadron launched, someone would point at the hill and tell the new kids the story: the year the tree flew, and what it carried.)

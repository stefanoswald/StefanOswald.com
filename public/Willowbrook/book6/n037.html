Night 37

The Tangle

Month 2: The Kite That Wouldn't Fly

This month's next exam: The tangle. Her line and Lily's, crossed in a gust, two kites, one knot, eighty feet up, both diamonds straining against each other like arguing dogs. The wrong moves, not made: no yanking (yanks tighten knots, "the fights and the tangles run on the same physics"). No blame ("the gust did it.

The wind is big and can't argue, but this time it actually was the wind, and it doesn't matter, the knot doesn't care whose fault"). The right moves, made: walk toward each other. Bring both kites down together, slow, four hands on two lines, the knot descending like a shared problem. And then: the bench, the knot in four laps' worth of string, one long hour of unpicking, talking the whole time, because unpicking is bench-work and bench-work runs on talk.

"A hour of knot for ten seconds of gust," Lily observed, winding her freed line. "Yeah. The tangles cost more than the tangling. Every time. In string and in people." A beat. "Worth learning on string, dough. String forgives. You can't always unpick a people-knot in a hour."

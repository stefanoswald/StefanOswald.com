Night 45

The Festival Eve

Month 2: The Kite That Wouldn't Fly

Festival eve. The willow kite: Finished. Hung in the garage beside the Hawk's rebuilt skeleton (the two aircraft, nose to nose, "the squadron's home hangar"). The final inspection, family-attended: the frame (bends, doesn't snap; dances, doesn't fight).

The bridle (three-times measured, adjustable, the most-fiddled part, this month's lesson, built in). The twelve tails, coiled like sleeping gold snakes. The leaf patch, veined and proud. And along the bottom edge, added tonight, small, in her best paint: four words.

Home. Family. Growing. Kind. ("The tree's whole sentence so far. If the willow's finally flying, its words fly too. That's not decoration. That's cargo.") The forecast: checked five times. ("Wind: eight to twelve, gusting fifteen.

The good numbers. The festival gods are kind, no. The festival weather is kind. Gods is a whole different notebook section.") And at the lamp: "Whatever happens tomorrow, prize or no prize, rip or no rip, the kite already did its whole job.

It taught me this month. The things you make teach you while you make them. The flying's just... the diploma."

Night 138

The Skeptic's Visit

Month 5: The Question Nobody Can Answer

This month met its counter-force at the court: a classmate, older-acting, armor-plated, running the old line: "Wonder's babyish. The stars are just gas. The dog's just a animal. The magic's not real.

Growing up means knowing better." The response, run on seven years of quill-theory (the armor read first: the file says his house runs on don't-be-childish; the wonder got priced out of him early; the cynicism is a quill coat, worn where the soft got laughed at): no debate. The crack, opened gently, with the one tool armor can't parry, a question in his own currency: "Okay. The stars are gas.

True, the answer shelf agrees. Here's my question, gas-scientist: why does the gas make light? And why does the light travel four hundred years and land in your eye, exact, tonight, out of the whole sky? You don't have to call it magic.

Call it physics. The physics is the most unanswerable thing out there, ask any real scientist; they're the wonderingest people alive; the knowing-better people aren't the scientists, they're the people who stopped at the labels. Gas. Animal.

Just. 'Just' is the stopping word. The real knowing-better is knowing there's no bottom. You want to grow up fastest? Out-wonder everybody. The wonder's not the baby part. The wonder's the engine." (The crack, sure enough, took: he showed up at the wonder table within this month, armor on, question smuggled under it: "why do cats purr.

The real answer. Nobody knows, right?" Nobody knows. He almost smiled. The quills, as ever, were guarding a soft that never went anywhere.)

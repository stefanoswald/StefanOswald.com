Night 289

The Re-Gift Law

Month 10: The Gift Month

A law defense at the workshop table (a visiting kid, scandalized: "you'd give somebody a used thing?!" the made-not-bought lesson meeting the town's oldest river): The re-gift ruling. "Used? The bee costume's on its fifth kid. The educated bikes carry their maps frame to frame.

The cut-down rod went Sam-to-Joe-to-me-to-Theo-pending. The red daypack. The whittling knife. The brass glass. Fifty years old when it reached me, and it came with a leaf-burner's whole story attached.

These aren't used things, everybody. These are educated things, pre-loaded with maps and stories and forty years of somebody's Saturdays. A new thing knows nothing. A handed thing knows the whole route. The hand-me-down river is the gift economy's deepest channel: it's the made-gift's three weeks, compounded, every previous keeper's weeks, riding along.

You don't re-gift because you're cheap. You re-gift because some things are too educated to retire. The storying at the handoff is the whole law, nothing leaves unstoried, and a storied handoff beats a store's whole shelf. Check the treasure boxes of this entire town: they're all hand-me-down rivers, frozen mid-flow. The river is the town. The town is a thing that gets handed."

Night 265

The Voice in the Letter

Month 9: Brave Out Loud

This month documented its last register: The written stand. (The occasion: a district survey on the exchange program, funding review; the town's whole knowing-exchange weighed by a committee two towns over, on forms. The forms had no box for what the exchange is. The voice, needed at a venue it couldn't attend, the committee meets in daytime, far away, grown-ups-only.)

The stand, written instead (the marks travel where the body can't; the whole postal law, aimed at bureaucracy): "To the committee: Your form asks for attendance numbers. Attached. It doesn't ask: Who taught the mending table (A widow who stopped building usses till the table gave her harold's trade back). Who learned the quiet.

What the reverse tables build (The elders get looked at hard by somebody trying to get them right. The attention, visible). Your form measures the jar it knows. The exchange is a shelf. Come count the shelf in person.

Tuesdays. The table's got room. Forms always do their best work as invitations. O. Age 7, co-founder, properly formatted." (The committee, later with satisfaction, came. Two members, one Tuesday, counted the shelf in person, and the funding renewed with an increase and a commendation that quoted the letter's last line.

Forms do their best work as invitations. So do stands. So does everything she ever wrote.)

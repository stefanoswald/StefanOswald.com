Night 154

The Story of the War Years

Month 6: Grandpa Joe's Slow Stories

Slow story two, the one the almanac holds in its thinnest entries, told at last at porch pace: The war years. (His father's generation; the brothers who left; the fields held four years by his grandmother and the wives..." The county's whole harvest, brought in by women the history books forgot to mention. The almanac didn't forget.

Look. "the 1943 page; the different hand; his grandmother's: "Brought in the corn. All of it. The girls and me. Let the story show." Let the story show. It shows. Both records. Forever.) "And the one who came home changed, my uncle Wendell.

Quiet went into him over there and never all the way out. The farm's medicine was the only kind he'd take: the milking, dawn and dusk, every day, forty years. The cows don't ask what you saw. The routine holds what the man can't say.

He was the gentlest hand with animals this county ever produced, the hurt ones somehow know each other, darlin'. The Duchess would've sat on his boot in a week." ("The routine holds what the man can't say..." She wrote slow.

"The farm was the first kit, Grandpa. The whole kit, the counting, the tending, the quiet, the things-that-don't-ask. Somebody should tell the doctors. Somebody has, darlin'. They're catching up. The farms knew first. The farms always know first.")

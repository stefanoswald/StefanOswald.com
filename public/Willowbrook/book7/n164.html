Night 164

The Story of the Town's Debt

Month 6: Grandpa Joe's Slow Stories

Slow story six, extracted over protest (his modesty thought it; the collection won): The town's debt. ("The boots man pays back a ghost. The valley saw to your hail winter. This month wants the other column, Grandpa: what did this farm see to?")

The column, assembled from his reluctant fragments plus the almanac's quiet corners: the mill closure winters ("eggs and milk ran to six houses on credit nobody wrote down. The book says 'Extended terms.' The terms were: pay never, mention never"). The depression his father farmed through ("the tramps knew the barn door's chalk mark, the whole hobo code; this farm's mark meant hot meal, no questions.

Fifty years of strangers fed. The mark's still on the door. Look next Saturday. I never painted it over. Some signs stay posted"). And the seed loans ("half the gardens in that town started with Honeywell seed, 'borrowed.'

Seeds pay demselves back by growing, darlin'. The whole lending library of the dirt"). ("The second grid's got a farm branch, Grandpa. Older than the bakery's. The chalk mark is the somebody wall, eighty years early.

Every generation thinks it invented the kindness, darlin'. The kindness just keeps hiring new hands. Now put the pencil down and hand me the catalog. The modesty's had all it can take for one porch.")

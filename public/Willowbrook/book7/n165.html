Night 165

The Slow Craft Lesson

Month 6: Grandpa Joe's Slow Stories

This month's hands-lesson: Whittling. (The porch craft; his father's knife, cut-down handle, the farm cuts everything down to the next hands; the fleet law in steel. Basswood blanks. Both grandchildren, blade-safety sworn, thumbs braced by the rule.)

The teaching, at porch pace: "Whittling's the one craft that's all taking away, darlin'. The bird's in the wood, your job is removing everything that isn't bird. Small cuts. With the grain. And the rule that runs the whole craft..."

The shaving curled; the bird emerged by subtraction. "You can't put wood back. Every cut's a spent coin. So the craft is nine parts looking to one part cutting. The lookers make birds. The hasty make toothpicks."

(Her first bird: recognizable; one wing thin..." Spent that coin hasty; saved. "Theo's: "a cow..." He declared, of a shape the story generously logs as bovine-adjacent. "a lying-down cow," the five-year-old's save; the family let it stand; it stands on his shelf yet.

And the lesson under the lesson, saved at the lamp: "The whittling's the whole month, Mama. The slow stories are Grandpa whittling the fifty years, taking away everything that isn't the bird. This month is nine parts looking. The bird's almost out.")

Night 175

The Month's Last Slow Story

Month 6: Grandpa Joe's Slow Stories

This month's last slow story, teller's choice, and he chose, as it happened, the one nobody saw coming: Hers. From his side. ("You told me the shelf from inside it. This month closes with the view from the porch:") "Seven years ago a baby came to this porch and looked at everything, the first thing anybody ever said about you, darlin', and it was your papa, at the window, day one: 'she looks.'

And the porch watched the looking become asking become keeping. I watched a two-year-old greet my meanest hen like a colleague-in-waiting. I watched a four-year-old read me the catalog slow, finger under the words, and hand me back forty years of Margaret's porch hours like she'd found them in a drawer. I watched the counting become the love, you think you learned that here.

Darlin', you brought it. The farm just gave it acreage. And the fifty-first year's best crop, the almanac will say it plainer when its day comes, but the porch says it now, at pace, in the gold: the best thing this farm ever grew came up two towns over, in a nursery, under a gold lamp, and drove out saturdays. The farm knows its harvests.

I'm looking at the crown one." (the story does not summarize her face. Some pages are for the attending. The porch held. The gold held. This month closed at the exact pace it opened: slow, true, and rich beyond all check.)

Night 168

The Story She Told Him

Month 6: Grandpa Joe's Slow Stories

This month reversed its current for one Sunday: She told the slow story. Hers. The seven years, told back to their oldest listener, porch-pace, whole: the story summarizes what it cannot improve: she told him the shelf. The gold light and the first Maybe.

The duck that earned the first word. The meadow's opening. The green room winter. The letters turning on. The school gates. The second grid. The insides year. The gate year, running now. Two hours, the catalog closed, the rocker still, the stillest the story has ever logged him, and at the end, the four-sentence man produced, from somewhere deeper than the porch, a fifth sentence's worth of response: "Darlin', I've heard fifty years of stories on this porch and told most of them.

That's the first time anybody ever handed me back a whole childhood with the threads still showing, the way the weather sees it, not the way the photos flatten it. Margaret used to say the listening was her half of the farming. I never fully took her meaning till just now. Sixty years married to the meaning and it took a seven-year-old's telling to land it.

Write that in your book: The meanings take the time they take. Some land in the telling. Some land sixty years after the teller. She'd have liked your telling past all sense, by the way.

But I've said that before. Some sentences bear repeating. That's the other thing the porch knows."

Night 178

The Question He Asked Her

Month 6: Grandpa Joe's Slow Stories

This month's last reversal, his question, for her, asked at the gold hour, plain: "The gate year, darlin'. The whole town feels it coming, the tree's last word, the thing the bear's walking you to, the shelf ending. My question, from the porch: Are you scared?" The answer, given honestly, with both sides included: "Some.

The good kind, the rope kind, the first-day kind: scared-plus-prepared, which is excited with homework. The shelf ending is... the bottoms-of-slides; I stitched it myself; I believe my own wall. And the gate, the gate I'm not scared of at all, and that's the strange part worth saying at this porch: everything else I ever faced, the fear came standard. The gate just comes with...

Readiness. Like the fear already got spent, in pieces, over seven years, every furnished dark, every crossed rope, every named feeling, like the whole shelf was the fear, paid in installments, and the gate's already paid for. Is that how it works, Grandpa? The long preparations eat the fear before the door even opens?"

("That's exactly how it works..." said the man three seasons from his own completed volume, rocking gold, entirely unafraid. "That's exactly how it works, darlin'. The long preparations eat the fear. The door opens on a paid account.

I'd know." The porch held. The gold held. The two paid accounts rocked side by side till the gloaming came, and neither was scared, and both were rich, and the story kept it all.)

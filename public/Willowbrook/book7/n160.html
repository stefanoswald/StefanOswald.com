Night 160

The Story of the Best Day

Month 6: Grandpa Joe's Slow Stories

The request, made at the porch by the collection's method: "The best day, Grandpa. Of the whole fifty years. This month should hold it." The big think ran a full rocking minute. The candidates, considered aloud: the willow yes.

The wedding. The children's births. The story harvests. And the winner, arriving with the force of a thing the teller himself hadn't known till asked: "A Tuesday. October, maybe fifteen years back. Nothing happened." (The porch waited; this month knows this shape; this month has been this shape:) "Margaret was in the kitchen.

The radio low. I'd fixed the gate that morning, the rusted hinge-pin, and the rain came at four, and I stood in the doorway with the coffee, and she hummed at the stove, and the whole farm was... Held, darlin'. Every animal fed.

Every fence standing. Every person I loved warm and near and humming. And I remember thinking. I wrote it down; you've read the entry; you didn't know it was the best one. I remember thinking: 'this is the whole of it, right here, and I'm in it.'

Fifty years, thousands of days, and the crown goes to a nothing-Tuesday I almost didn't notice." The rock, slow. "Almost didn't notice. That's the story's whole cargo, and it's the only inheritance worth a fence post: The best days wear ordinary clothes. Catch them live, darlin'. The catching is the whole religion. Everything else is commentary."

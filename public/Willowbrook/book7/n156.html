Night 156

The Story He Almost Didn't Tell

Month 6: Grandpa Joe's Slow Stories

Slow story four came at the brook, not the porch. Joe walked her to the boyhood pools for it, because some stories need their ground: The brother. ("You didn't know I had one. Almost nobody living does.

Samuel. Two years older. These were our pools, the cut-down rod was his before it was mine before it was yours.") The story, told to the water: the fever, 1949. Eleven and nine. The winter the almanac's earliest pages don't mention because his father couldn't write it and never did.

"Sixty-some years, darlin', and I've said his name out loud maybe a dozen times. Not because the missing faded. Because the saying had nowhere to go, everybody who knew him went quiet or went on. And then this family started its ways, the retelling rescues, the office hours of the gone ones, the saying-is-the-keeping, and I watched you keep the Duchess alive at every Saturday count and I thought: Samuel's been waiting sixty years for the family to learn what you all learned.

So." He cast the old rod, easy, the water taking the line. "Samuel. Sam. He laughed at everything. He'd have taught you the pools before I got the chance. He was the braver of us and I've spent sixty years being brave enough for two, and that arithmetic..."

The line drifted; the story held still. "that arithmetic is why the farm never scared me and the quiet never scared me and the only thing that ever scared me was a name going unsaid. It's said now. The story's got him. The records all got him now."

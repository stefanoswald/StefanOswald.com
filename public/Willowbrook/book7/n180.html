Night 180

The Slow Month, Counted

Month 6: Grandpa Joe's Slow Stories

Month's end. At month's end, The notebook could hold nine slow stories (the wagon and the spring; the war years and the women's harvest; the courting disasters and the four sentences that saved everything; samuel, the story remains; the name said; all the records got him now; the book's birth; the best nothing-Tuesday; the chalk mark still posted; Wendell's whistle, handed to the next gentlest hand; the view from the porch). One recipe archaeology (close; correctly close; the gap the size of the missing). One hard question, both directions.

One entry craft, transferred (the margins are for the meanings). One fence walk. One nap, unapologized. One quiet Sunday. One cloth volume. Beyond the numbers, there was that the slow was never the problem, the slow was the format.

That the body becomes the almanac eventually; everything becomes the book it kept. That the long preparations eat the fear, and the doors open on paid accounts. That the best days wear ordinary clothes, and the catching is the whole religion, and everything else is commentary. "Good slow-month?

The richest one that will ever fit in a month. And Mama, this month's last line, from the porch's whole teaching: I thought the slow stories were Grandpa giving me the past. They weren't. They were him giving me the pace.

The porch pace. The whittling pace. The nine-parts-looking. The whole fast world's gonna push me my whole life, and now there's a porch added in me, permanent, where the pace lives. The inheritance was never the stories.

The inheritance was the speed they get told at. Sit still, everybody. The long ones are worth it. The long ones are the only ones, in the end." Remembered. The porch is added. The pace is inherited. On we go, slowly, where it counts.

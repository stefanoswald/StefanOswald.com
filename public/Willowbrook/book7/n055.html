Night 55

The Question Answered at Kid-Scale

Month 2: Why Do Things End?

This month's exam arrived, by the pattern, from below: Theo, at his lamp, asking his edition: "Wivy. Why do things end? The summer ended. The corn ended. The baby teeth end, mine started wiggling; the upgrade comes."

(The briefing holds; three generations of it now.) "But why?" this month, compressed to five-year-old scale, delivered at his eye level by the eternal method: "Okay. The whole month, your size: Things end like the slide ends, Theo. The whoosh is the best part and the whoosh only works because the slide has a bottom.

A slide with no bottom isn't a longer whoosh, it's just falling. The bottom is what makes it a ride. And here's the other half: the bottom of the slide is where the ladder starts. You hit the bottom and you run to the ladder and you go again, that's the whole design.

Endings are the bottoms of slides. They make the ride a ride, and they're standing right next to the next ladder. Every time. Look for the ladder. It's always there." (He absorbed it the way he absorbs the big ones: whole, silent, one thumb in the old spot.

Then: "... The ladder wish. From my four candles. I wished a ladder, Wivy. The candle knew..." and the story sat all the way up, because the sealed wish, two years held, had just unsealed itself at exactly its moment, the way the good ones do.

The boy wished for ladders before he knew why. Tenderhearted. The whole family physics, confirmed again, at a nightlight.)
